## Executive Summary

Requirement #2 produced three categories of finding:

1. **v1.2 is largely accurate where it makes claims.** No fundamental contradictions surfaced between the rubric and Brandy's rater walkthrough. Multiple v1.2 claims were directly validated by Brandy's commentary on the L12 vs L9 contrast.

2. **v1.2 has three concrete gaps** that the calibration pass surfaced. Each is specific, named, and warrants a v1.3 edit:
   - Repetitive sentence-opener pattern (`there is / there seems / there also`) as a Level-9-ceiling marker, not currently captured
   - Sentence fragments treated as distinct from grammatically-complete simple sentences — current v1.2 collapses them
   - Categorical vagueness (`plenty of flavors`, `lots of activities`, `some sort of arts and crafts`) as a vocabulary marker distinct from generic-adjective imprecision

3. **The text-only Listenability scoring approach has methodology limits worse than v1.2 acknowledges.** The hybrid approach (text-derived proxies + audio markers from Whisper timing) is structurally insufficient to detect several penalty-bearing audible features that raters explicitly attend to. This is captured as an updated v1.2 → v1.3 entry in Section 18 (Open Gaps) and as a constraint on the v1 scorer's claimed accuracy.

The full punch list is in [Section 5](#5-v12--v13-edit-list-prioritized) below.

## 1. Methodology Note: How This Synthesis Was Built

The calibration scorecard was filled in a **hybrid mode** that wasn't anticipated when I designed it:

- The **transcript** (Brandy's voice + presentation walkthrough) was the source for ticking checkboxes about what the rater explicitly named, what vocabulary she praised, and what she identified as band-distinguishing markers.
- **Audio-only features** (pace, intonation contour, filler density beyond what Brandy quotes, raw pause length) were partially fillable from Brandy's *commentary on* those features ("a few more pauses," "rhythm was very fluent") but not from direct audio observation.

This produced two known artifacts in the ticked data that are addressed below rather than treated as errors:

- **Some checkboxes appear under the "wrong" band column from a raw-observation perspective.** For example, "multi-clause subordination" is ticked under L9, "mostly simple sentences strung with and/but/so" is ticked under L12. Reviewing the transcript: Brandy's L9 → L12 *revision* of the L9 response involved adding subordination, and her praise of L12 included structural variety. The ticks reflect features present in the *revised* L9 response after Brandy's rewrite, not the original. This isn't a mistake — it's evidence that subordination is precisely the feature that moves a response from L9 to L12, which is itself useful calibration data.
- **Sections 2–6 (pace/pauses/fillers/self-correction/intonation) are largely unticked.** This isn't because those features were absent. It's because they couldn't be honestly assessed without raw audio. The unticked state in those sections is a methodology finding, not a content finding.

The synthesis below treats Brandy's commentary as primary signal and the operator's transcript-mediated ticks as secondary corroboration.

## 2. v1.2 Validations (Things the Calibration Confirmed)

### 2.1 Listenability: pause cost is real and band-distinguishing

v1.2 Section 15 (Pause and Self-Correction Taxonomy) defines pause types and their score effects. Brandy validated this directly:

> The L9 speaker "had a few more pauses than our first test taker did so it slowed things down just a little bit." Critically, she frames this as a **content-density consequence** — the L9 transcript "is quite a bit shorter than the first test takers and that indicates that maybe his pauses could have been removed to afford more opportunity to describe the picture."

**Implication:** Pauses don't just lower Listenability — they cap Content/Coherence too, by eating speaking time. v1.2 treats these as separate criteria. The cross-criterion effect is worth flagging in the rubric.

### 2.2 Listenability: rhythm and energy are real audible signals

v1.2 lists "Rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation" as an Important Factor. Brandy's L12 analysis hit all three:

> "His pronunciation was clear his intonation I think was fine as well it was rising and falling and there was some energy and enthusiasm in the response as well."

The **"energy and enthusiasm"** specifically is a feature v1.2 names indirectly through "engaged with their own content" but doesn't elevate. Worth keeping in mind for v2 (audio-aware scorer).

### 2.3 Vocabulary: precision and idioms differentiate bands as v1.2 claims

v1.2 Section 8 says Level 12 vocabulary uses "a broad range of figures of speech and idioms" while Level 9 uses "some figures of speech and idioms." Brandy directly validated this with specific praise of L12 vocabulary:

> "Bunting, cyan, smoothie outfit" — three uncommon words.
> "Unfortunately" — an adverb adding a layer of description.
> "Straining at the leash, tasty kebab, does not look impressed, seems to be oblivious" — specifically called out "oblivious" as "a sophisticated word choice."

For L9, Brandy used the v1.2-aligned descriptor: vocabulary is "adequate" and "suitable" — exactly the Level 9 wording.

### 2.4 Content/Coherence: organization quality differentiates bands

v1.2 names "Organization of ideas" as an Important Factor for Content/Coherence. Brandy explicitly contrasted the two responses on this dimension:

> L12: "starts with what I would call the big picture and then he moves in to focus on individuals in the scene… each person leads into the the following person like a chain of events."
> L9: "he jumps around a little bit… so it makes it harder for a listener to follow."

Brandy then *demonstrated* the upgrade by reorganizing the L9 response into the same big-picture-to-detail flow. The organizational pattern is reproducible and teachable, which means it's also AI-scorable from text alone — strong validation that text-based Content/Coherence scoring will work.

### 2.5 Task Fulfillment: completeness and time use track v1.2 descriptors

The L12 speaker "finishes his response within the 60-second time limit" and the response is judged complete. The L9 speaker stopped early — Brandy noted his transcript was visibly shorter. v1.2 Section 4 explicitly states "Length is gated by Completeness" and the calibration validates this: shorter time use is penalized when it indicates incomplete coverage.

### 2.6 Native-speaker status is not a free pass to Level 12

A subtle but important calibration point. Brandy explicitly says:

> "Even though we might speak English as our first language it doesn't automatically guarantee a level 12 response."

Both speakers were native English speakers. One scored 12, one scored 9. The differentiator was **content development, organization, and vocabulary deployment** — not pronunciation, accent, or grammatical accuracy. This validates the Speaking Study Tip #7 ("Don't worry about your accent") that v1.2 already cites in Section 17, but adds calibration weight: scoring is about what you do with the language, not how native-sounding you are.

## 3. v1.2 Gaps (Things the Calibration Surfaced)

These are concrete additions warranted for v1.3, organized by where they belong.

### 3.1 GAP — Repetitive sentence-opener pattern as a Level-9-ceiling marker

**Where it belongs:** Section 9 (Listenability) and Section 13 (Per-Task TF Checklists) — Task 3 and Task 8.

**Brandy's framing:**

> "Look at how many times he starts his sentences with these words: 'there seems,' 'there is,' 'lots that there also'… we can do a lot better. We can take out the 'there' and replace it with a strong subject and a strong verb."

This is treated as a **primary** reason for the Level 9 cap on this response, not a minor issue. Brandy spent multiple paragraphs of her revision walkthrough showing how to eliminate the pattern.

**v1.2 currently misses this** because Section 9's Listenability descriptors talk about "variety of sentence structure" abstractly. The specific, audible, high-frequency "there is / there seems / there also" pattern as a sentence-opener default is a concrete marker that an AI scorer can detect from text alone with a regex.

**Why it matters for the AI scorer:** This is a high-precision, easy-to-detect text feature that maps directly to a band ceiling. Brandy's commentary suggests the rule is roughly: *if a response opens 4+ sentences with "there [is/are/seems/was/also/were]" out of 8–12 total sentences, that pattern alone caps the response at Level 9 regardless of other strengths.*

### 3.2 GAP — Sentence fragments distinct from grammatically-complete simple sentences

**Where it belongs:** Section 9 (Listenability — Grammar and sentence structure factor).

**Brandy's framing:**

> "The black underlined sentences are actually sentence fragments. These sentences are not complete — they're missing a complete subject and complete verb. So when we're speaking we still need to speak in complete sentences."

She explicitly distinguishes this from "simple sentences strung with and/but/so." A speaker can produce grammatically-complete simple sentences and still fall in the Level 6–8 range (acceptable simplicity). Producing **fragments** (missing subject or verb) is a different and worse error that specifically caps a response below Level 10.

**v1.2 currently treats grammar as a single dimension** with descriptors like "control of simple structures" without distinguishing complete-but-simple from fragmentary. Adding the fragment-vs-simple distinction makes the AI scorer more precise.

**Why it matters for the AI scorer:** Fragment detection is mechanically straightforward in text — sentences without main verbs, dependent clauses standing alone, noun phrases without finite verbs. Adding this as a specific check sharpens grammar scoring substantially.

### 3.3 GAP — Categorical vagueness as a vocabulary marker distinct from generic adjectives

**Where it belongs:** Section 8 (Vocabulary) and Section 14 (Vocabulary Upgrade Techniques).

**Brandy's framing:**

> "The smoothie stand does have plenty of flavors but our speaker never identifies what any of those flavors are…
> 'lots of activities happening' — well, what activities? Let's be specific…
> 'some sort of arts and crafts' — if he's going to mention arts and crafts then perhaps he can give some examples."

This is **abstract noun-phrase vagueness without concrete instantiation** — a specific pattern that's neither a generic-adjective issue (which v1.2 covers via Technique 3 in Section 14) nor an idiom issue. It's a vocabulary-deployment issue: using categorical nouns ("flavors," "activities," "arts and crafts") without populating them with specifics.

**v1.2 currently has Technique 3 ("Replace Generic Adjectives") which addresses good→excellent, big→well-established.** The vagueness pattern is different — it's about expanding a categorical noun into instances, not upgrading an adjective.

**Why it matters for the AI scorer:** Detecting this requires looking for category-noun + nothing-following patterns. Specifically: "X different Y" / "lots of Y" / "some sort of Y" / "many Y" without any subsequent enumeration. This is detectable from text and is a strong Level-9-ceiling signal.

### 3.4 GAP — Tolerance-for-uncorrected-errors at Level 12

**Where it belongs:** Section 7 (Content/Coherence — Level 12 descriptor) and Section 18 (calibration anchor for borderline scoring).

**Brandy's framing:**

> "It's a comical error — clearly we understand that it's the owner. I think what happened is our speaker reversed these words… I point that out to show you that we're not looking for perfection on any selpip response. Even with little tiny mistakes like this, as you can see it's still very possible to achieve that level 12. We knew what he meant and it did not hurt the overall understanding of his response."

The L12 speaker said "the owner's dog is wearing a pair of green shorts" — a semantic mix-up where the dog became the wearer of the shorts. Brandy explicitly forgave this because comprehensibility was preserved.

**v1.2 currently lacks this calibration anchor.** The closest relevant content is the existing Gap 4 entry which gives examples of what tips a borderline case in either direction. This adds a new anchor: *isolated semantic slips are forgivable at Level 12 if (a) the listener can recover the intended meaning from context, (b) the slip is genuinely isolated, not a pattern.*

**Why it matters for the AI scorer:** Without this anchor, the scorer will over-penalize Level 12 responses for any semantic error, capping them at Level 11. The anchor lets the scorer apply leniency where the error is recoverable.

### 3.5 GAP — Pronunciation slips noticed but not band-lowering

**Where it belongs:** Section 9 (Listenability — Pronunciation factor).

**Brandy's framing:**

> The L9 speaker pronounced "decal" as "deckle." Brandy noted it but did **not** treat it as a band-affecting issue. "Overall, I mean pronunciation is very strong."

**v1.2 currently has the Speaking Study Tip #7** ("Don't worry about your accent") but doesn't operationalize this for the scorer. The calibration adds a specific anchor: *individual word mispronunciations don't lower Listenability if (a) the rest of pronunciation is clear, (b) the word is recognizable from context.*

This is mostly relevant for v2 (multimodal audio scorer). For v1's text-only scorer, this is a non-issue — the scorer can't detect mispronunciation anyway. Capturing it now prevents v2 from over-engineering pronunciation detection.

### 3.6 GAP — Adverbs as a vocabulary-range signal

**Where it belongs:** Section 8 (Vocabulary) and Section 14 (Technique 3).

**Brandy's framing:**

> "[The L12 speaker is] even throwing in adverbs — so that word 'unfortunately' just gives us an added layer of description."

**v1.2 currently emphasizes adjectives, verbs, and idioms** as vocabulary-range markers. Adverbs aren't called out as a distinct signal. Adding "presence of evaluative or descriptive adverbs" to the v1.2 vocabulary markers gives the scorer one more signal of range.

**Why it matters for the AI scorer:** Adverb detection is mechanically straightforward in text (most end in -ly; common evaluative ones are listable). This is a small but easy enhancement.

### 3.7 GAP — Big-picture-to-small-picture as a Task 3 organizational pattern

**Where it belongs:** Section 6 (Four Organization Types — Organization 2) and Section 13 (Per-Task TF — Task 3).

**Brandy's framing:**

> "Our test taker has decided to start with what I would call the big picture and then he moves in to focus on individuals in the scene… each person leads into the following person like a chain of events. Our speaker is not just popping all over the image and picking random hidden people."

v1.2 Section 6 already names "general statement → details → connections" as the Organization 2 pattern for Tasks 3 and 4. The calibration adds a more specific scoring criterion: **"connections" includes a chained, sequential walkthrough of subjects, not random reference**. The L9 response had connections (some prepositions, some details) but lacked the *chaining* — speaker jumped between food stalls in an order that didn't match their visual layout, then jumped to people, back to objects.

This is a refinement of an existing v1.2 marker, not a new one. It belongs as a sub-bullet under Section 6 Organization 2 and as a TF checklist item for Task 3 and Task 4.

## 4. Methodology Findings (About the AI Scoring Approach Itself)

These aren't rubric edits. They're updates to how confident we should be in the v1 scorer.

### 4.1 Text-only Listenability scoring is more limited than v1.2 acknowledges

**v1.2 Section 9 already concedes** that two of Listenability's four Important Factors (rhythm/pronunciation/intonation, pauses) are audio-dependent. The calibration showed a stronger version of this:

- Brandy's commentary on **why one response scored higher** repeatedly invokes audible features the scorer cannot directly measure.
- The L9 speaker's pause-induced content shortage is a *cross-criterion* effect (Listenability + Content/Coherence) that text scoring will systematically underestimate.
- Pronunciation slips that Brandy noticed but did not penalize ("deckle" for "decal") are the easy case. The hard case — pronunciation slips that DO penalize because they impede understanding — would be undetectable to the v1 text-only scorer.

**Update for v1:** the scorer's disclosed limits should explicitly say it may overestimate Listenability for responses with significant pause/pronunciation issues. This is consistent with the existing v1.2 disclosure approach but should be sharpened.

### 4.2 The v1.2 hybrid approach (Whisper timing data + text features) helps but doesn't close the gap

v1.2 Section 15 plans for the AI scorer to use Whisper word timestamps to infer pause length and filler density. The calibration suggests this is necessary but insufficient:

- **Whisper timing catches pause length** — useful for Section 15's 3+ second long-pause flag.
- **Whisper transcription catches fillers** — useful for the uh/um/eh/hmm/err/ah counter.
- **Whisper does NOT catch:** rhythm variation, intonation contour, energy/enthusiasm, mid-word pauses (which Whisper renders as one word), confidence in voice.

For v1, this is acceptable as long as it's disclosed. For v2, multimodal audio scoring is the only path to genuine Listenability accuracy.

### 4.3 The L9 response could be revised to L12 in a single editing pass

Brandy demonstrated this live. Starting from the L9 transcript, she:
1. Reorganized content (food stalls together, then people)
2. Combined fragments into complex sentences using subordinating conjunctions
3. Replaced categorical vagueness with specific instantiations
4. Added adverbs and descriptive phrases
5. Eliminated repetitive sentence openers

The revised response is a Level 12 response. This is **strong evidence that the v1.2 Vocabulary Upgrade Techniques (Section 14) are the right framework** — the same set of moves that Brandy demonstrated. It also suggests the AI scorer's "How to Improve" feedback should follow Brandy's revision template directly.

**Update for v1:** the "How to Improve" feature can be marketed as "Same revision template Paragon uses in their official teaching materials" — that's a defensible accuracy claim grounded in this calibration.

## 6. Closing Requirement #2

Requirement #2 was set up as YouTube ear calibration with a 60-minute budget. The actual execution was different but produced equivalent value:

- **What was originally planned:** Watch 25–60 min of CELPIP video, validate v1.2 against audio observations.
- **What actually happened:** A hybrid pass — transcript-based extraction of explicit rater commentary, scorecard-mediated structuring of observations, methodology critique of the scorecard itself.

The hybrid pass is arguably stronger than the original plan because Brandy's rater commentary is a higher-fidelity signal than untrained-ear observation. The trade-off is that audio-only features (raw rhythm, intonation contour, mid-utterance pauses) remain genuinely uncalibrated — a known v2 concern.

**Status:** Requirement #2 closed. v1.2 → v1.3 punch list produced with 9 specific edits ranked by priority and effort. Recommended next step: defer v1.3 production until after MVP build. The current v1.2 is already strong enough to start prompt engineering against; the v1.3 edits will sharpen the scorer but aren't blocking.

**What replaces "ear calibration" in the v1 plan going forward:** beta-tester feedback. Once 5–10 paying users have real CELPIP scores to compare against the AI scorer's outputs, calibration will happen against actual ground truth. That's a higher-fidelity calibration signal than any amount of YouTube watching.
