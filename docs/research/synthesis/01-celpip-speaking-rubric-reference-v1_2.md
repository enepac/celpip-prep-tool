## 2. Test Structure and Context

### CELPIP-General Test Overview

CELPIP-General is a computer-delivered English proficiency test designed by Paragon Testing Enterprises (a subsidiary of UBC). It is one of two designated English language tests for Canadian permanent residency (Express Entry and other immigration streams) and Canadian citizenship.

The test consists of four components delivered in a single 3-hour sitting:

| Component | Duration | Tasks |
|---|---|---|
| Listening | 47–55 min | 6 parts, 38 questions + 1 unscored practice |
| Reading | 55–60 min | 4 parts, 38 questions + 1 unscored practice |
| Writing | 53–60 min | 2 tasks (email + survey response) |
| **Speaking** | **15–20 min** | **8 scored tasks + 1 unscored practice** |

### Speaking Component Specifics

- All 8 Speaking tasks are scored
- One unscored practice task precedes the scored tasks (optional but recommended for voice warm-up)
- Each task involves an on-screen prompt with preparation time, followed by a timed recorded response
- The test is delivered entirely by computer — no human examiner is present during recording
- Speaking is the **last** component of the CELPIP Test
- When prep time is up, the screen automatically advances and a "Start speaking now" prompt initiates recording
- Test-takers are advised to speak at normal volume — too loud or too soft negatively affects Listenability scoring
- Background noise in the room does not affect rater audibility provided the speaker uses normal volume

### The "Everyday English" Principle (from Performance Standards webinar)

CELPIP is explicitly designed around **everyday English used in ordinary day-to-day situations** — not academic or literary English. Test-takers who use over-formal, textbook-style language sometimes score *lower* because the register feels unnatural. The cheeseburger sentence is Paragon's canonical anchor:

> *"I'd really like a medium-well cheeseburger with fries and a glass of cola with a little ice, two straws, and a wedge of lemon on the side, please."*

This single ordinary utterance contains all four scoring dimensions: precise vocabulary (*medium-well*, *wedge*), supporting details (modifiers across the sentence), grammar/structure variety, and appropriate tone (*please*). The takeaway: high band scores come from doing ordinary English very well, not from forcing complex or academic language.

### Scoring and Acceptance

- CELPIP scores are accepted by **Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC)** for permanent residency and citizenship applications
- Designated by **the Australian Government's Department of Home Affairs (DHA)** for visa purposes
- Accepted for professional designations in Canada
- Accepted for the **Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP)**
- Online results in 3-4 business days
- Test fee: $290 + taxes (CELPIP-General); $195 + taxes (CELPIP-General LS, citizenship-only)

## 3. Scoring Scale and CLB/CEFR Mapping

Each Speaking response is scored on the four criteria independently. The composite Speaking score is reported on the CELPIP scale (0-12), which is calibrated to the Canadian Language Benchmark (CLB) levels.

### Full Scoring Table

| CELPIP Level | CLB Level | CEFR | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|---|
| 12 | 12 | C2 | Expert proficiency in high-stakes social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 11 | 11 | C1 | Advanced proficiency in high-stakes social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 10 | 10 | C1 | Highly effective proficiency in some high-stakes social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 9 | 9 | B2 | Effective proficiency in some high-stakes social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 8 | 8 | B2 | Good proficiency in more demanding social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 7 | 7 | B2 | Adequate proficiency in somewhat demanding social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 6 | 6 | B1 | Developing proficiency in everyday social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 5 | 5 | B1 | Acquiring proficiency in everyday social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 4 | 4 | A2 | Adequate proficiency for daily life activities |
| 3 | 3 | A2 | Some proficiency in limited contexts of personal relevance |
| 2 | 1, 2 | A1 | Limited ability in contexts related to immediate needs |
| 1 | / | / | Insufficient information to assess |
| 0 | / | / | Insufficient information to assess |
| NA | / | / | Not Administered |

### Why CLB 7, 9, and 10 Matter Most

For Canadian immigration purposes (Express Entry — the primary use case for CELPIP candidates):

- **CLB 7** — Threshold for Federal Skilled Worker eligibility; minimum for many PR streams
- **CLB 9** — Triggers full Express Entry CRS points for language; the most common target
- **CLB 10** — Maximum Express Entry CRS points for language ability (along with CLB 11+)

This document covers Levels 5-12 because that is the working zone for the target user population. Levels below 5 are out of scope for v1.

### How Composite Scoring Works

Each Speaking response is scored separately on each of the four criteria. The final reported CELPIP Level is a product of scores across all 8 tasks. There are many ways to achieve a particular Level — strong performance on some tasks/criteria can offset weaker performance on others. This means our AI's per-criterion scoring matters more than getting any single composite score perfect.

## 4. The Official Performance Standards

This is the rater-side scoring framework — the four "guiding questions" CELPIP raters use, with the specific factors they evaluate. Source: CELPIP Speaking Performance Standards (Prometric, © 2023). This framework is the most condensed and actionable rubric available; it should be the primary lens for AI scoring.

### Performance Standards Table

| Dimension | Important Factors | Guiding Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Content/Coherence** | • Number of ideas<br>• Quality of ideas<br>• Organization of ideas<br>• Examples and supporting details | How well are the ideas organized and developed? |
| **Vocabulary** | • Word choice<br>• Suitable use of words and phrases<br>• Range of words and phrases<br>• Precision and accuracy | What is the range of the test taker's vocabulary and can they use it naturally? |
| **Listenability** | • Rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation<br>• Pauses, interjections, and self-correction<br>• Grammar and sentence structure<br>• Variety of sentence structure | How easy is it to listen to and understand the response? |
| **Task Fulfillment** | • Relevance<br>• Completeness<br>• Tone<br>• Length | How well did the test taker follow the instructions? Did they use an appropriate tone? |

### How to Use This Framework for AI Scoring

For each criterion, the AI should evaluate the response against the four Important Factors and produce reasoning grounded in the Guiding Question. The factors are not independent dimensions to be scored separately — they are facets of the same criterion that should be weighed together.

**Critical insight from the rater-side rubric:** Listenability includes "Grammar and sentence structure" and "Variety of sentence structure" as Important Factors. This means grammar and structural variety are *part of Listenability scoring*, not separate concerns. A text-only AI scoring system can therefore evaluate Listenability meaningfully by analyzing transcripts for grammatical complexity and structural variety, even without audio analysis. This significantly strengthens v1's ability to score Listenability from text alone.

**Critical insight on Task Fulfillment "Length":** Length is an Important Factor, but it is **gated by Completeness**. From the Performance Standards webinar: a short response that fully addresses every sub-task can still score well, but a response that runs out of time before covering all sub-tasks is penalized for both completeness and length. Speakers must use the allotted time to demonstrate proficiency, but cutting off content mid-task is a worse error than finishing a few seconds early.

## 5. The Eight Speaking Tasks — Timing and Format

Source: CELPIP Speaking Pro: Target 9+ Study Pack and CELPIP Guidebook for Test Takers.

### Master Timing Table

| Task | Communicative Function | Prep Time | Speaking Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Practice Task | Voice warm-up (unscored, optional) | 30 sec | 60 sec |
| Task 1: Giving Advice | Recommend a course of action | 30 sec | **90 sec** |
| Task 2: Talking about a Personal Experience | Narrate a past event | 30 sec | 60 sec |
| Task 3: Describing a Scene | Describe a picture in detail | 30 sec | 60 sec |
| Task 4: Making Predictions | Predict what happens next in the same picture | 30 sec | 60 sec |
| Task 5: Comparing and Persuading | Compare options and persuade (2 parts) | 60 + 60 sec | 0 + 60 sec |
| Task 6: Dealing with a Difficult Situation | Explain a decision in a tough interpersonal context | **60 sec** | 60 sec |
| Task 7: Expressing Opinions | Defend an opinion on a topic | 30 sec | **90 sec** |
| Task 8: Describing an Unusual Situation | Describe an unfamiliar scene/object | 30 sec | 60 sec |

**Notes on timing variations:**

- **Tasks 1 and 7** get 90 seconds of speaking time (longer than the others) — these tasks expect more developed reasoning
- **Tasks 5 and 6** get 60 seconds of prep time (longer than the others) — these tasks involve more cognitive load (comparison/persuasion and difficult situations)
- **Task 5 has two parts:** Part 1 is silent prep (read, understand, choose); Part 2 is the spoken response

### Task-Agnostic Note on Rubric Application

The four scoring criteria apply across all 8 task types. A CLB 9 vocabulary range looks the same in Task 1 as in Task 7. What changes between tasks is **the communicative situation** — what counts as fulfilling the task depends on what the task asked for. This is why Section 13 (Per-Task Task Fulfillment Checklists) is essential: relevance and completeness are task-specific even when the rubric is task-agnostic.

## 6. The Four Organization Types

Source: Scoring and Performance Standards webinar (Paragon). The 8 tasks group into 4 organizational patterns. This grouping is more useful for teaching and for AI prompt structure than treating the 8 tasks as fully independent.

### Organization 1 — Tasks 1 and 2 (Sequential / Chronological)

**Pattern:** Multiple suggestions or events delivered in sequence, signaled by ordering transitions.

**Transition templates:**
- "First of all…"
- "Next…"
- "Another thing you should do…"
- "It might be worth considering…"
- (Closing) "Well, I hope that helps you. Good luck on your first day!" — the closing-with-encouragement pattern is canonical for Task 1

**For Task 2 (narrative):** Time-anchor transitions:
- "In the morning, we met at…"
- "Then we went to…"
- "After that, I took them to…"
- "Finally, I took them to…"
- "By the end of the day…"

### Organization 2 — Tasks 3 and 4 (General Statement → Details → Connections)

**Pattern:** Establish overview, then provide specific details, then connect elements.

**Three-step template:**
1. **Start with a general statement** — establish the setting in one sentence ("This is a busy post office. There are people of all ages.")
2. **Provide details** — focus on specific people, objects, or actions (don't try to describe everything)
3. **Make connections** — link people/objects to each other ("the woman with the long hair *next to* the man with parcels")

**Spatial vocabulary bank for Tasks 3 and 4:**
- *Phrases:* "to the right of these two", "next to him", "at the counter", "just to her right"
- *Prepositions:* to, at, next to, between, below, beside, in front of, behind, under, to the right of

**Task 4 specific:** "Be clear which person, people, or things your prediction is about." Pronoun-only references ("he will probably…") without identifying who *he* is hurt clarity. Level 9+ predictions name the person before predicting ("The boy on the far left side of the picture is going to give the money to the woman with the long hair").

### Organization 3 — Tasks 5 and 6 (Preference → Introduce Each Point → Support)

**Pattern:** State your choice, then walk through points one by one with explicit transitions.

**Template:**
1. **State preference** — "I think the bed and breakfast is a much better choice for us."
2. **Introduce each point with transitions:**
   - "First of all, when we look at cost versus value…"
   - "The sleeping arrangement is much more comfortable at the B and B."
   - "We also need to consider the toilet situation."
   - "The B and B also offers the convenience of a hot breakfast."

**Task 5-specific prep scaffold (Main Points of Comparison grid):**

Walk through each comparable feature side by side during prep:

| Feature | Option A | Option B |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | $235/night | $120/night → cheaper |
| Bedrooms | shared | private, more comfortable |
| Bathrooms | portable toilet | private, more convenient |
| Kitchen/breakfast | small kitchen, no food | small kitchen + hot breakfast included |

Then synthesize by feature in the response, not by option.

**Task 6 specific tone language (softening phrases):**
- "I hope that you can understand that…"
- "I think we can all agree that…"
- "Let's try to look at it this way…"

### Organization 4 — Tasks 7 and 8 (Main Idea → Reasons → Examples → Transitions)

**Pattern:** Outline-driven response with main idea, supporting reasons, specific examples.

**Task 7 prep scaffold (Main Idea / Supporting Detail two-column grid):**

| Main Idea | Supporting Detail |
|---|---|
| Travel expands minds | Makes people more tolerant of others |
| Travel exposes you to new ways of doing things | Learn new methods that could help in career |
| Travel = first-hand experience about places | Not just reading about places |

**Task 7/8 transition vocabulary:**
- "First of all…"
- "In addition…"
- "Also…"
- "Finally…"

**Tasks 3 vs 8 distinction (within Organization 4 vs Organization 2):** Both describe a picture, but:
- **Task 3** = familiar/ordinary scene, uses Org 2 (general → details → connections)
- **Task 8** = strange/surprising, demands *precise details* and uses Org 4 (description must be richer because the listener cannot reconstruct the unusual content from generic language)

## 7. Criterion 1 — Content/Coherence

**Definition:** The speaker's ability to organize ideas logically, support claims with reasoning, and present information with appropriate complexity for the situation.

**Guiding Question (rater-side):** *How well are the ideas organized and developed?*

**Important Factors:**
- Number of ideas
- Quality of ideas
- Organization of ideas
- Examples and supporting details

### Level 12 — Expert Proficiency

**Descriptor (what speakers can do):**
- Communicate in **demanding non-routine** situations for a **full range of purposes**, intentions, and objectives
- Present information and develop ideas with **complex, clear, and precise** descriptions or details

**Rater Analysis (from celpip.ca Task A and Task B samples):**

> "Speakers can communicate in demanding non-routine situations for a full range of purposes, intentions, and objectives. In Task A, the speaker lessens the certainty of some statements (e.g., 'You *probably* would not be able to get a salary like this here in Canada') and strengthens other statements to indicate more certainty (e.g., 'So, you would *always* be able to come and visit your family')."

> "Speakers are also able to present information and develop ideas with complex, clear, and precise descriptions or details. In Task B, the speaker provides clear and precise details when stating, 'I should note that on the back of the chair, there's a switch to control the reading light above your head.'"

**Observable markers for AI scoring at Level 12:**
- Modulates certainty with precision (distinguishing "probably," "always," "definitely" by context)
- Provides specific, layered descriptions
- Addresses multiple communicative purposes within a single response
- Ideas connect with logical sophistication
- From Target 9+ Task 4B sample: includes opening and closing, develops main ideas with details, organizes response logically

### Level 11 — Advanced Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Communicate in demanding **non-routine** situations for a **range of purposes**
- Present information and develop ideas with **clear and precise** descriptions or details

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can communicate effectively in demanding non-routine situations for a range of purposes. In Task B, the speaker performs successfully in this unusual situation by selecting words and phrases to describe the strange object and explaining how a picture of it will be sent to the listener."

> "Speakers can present information and develop ideas with precise descriptions or details. In Task A, the speaker provides several ideas and develops them with precise details such as the need to research the culture to 'see if you would be able to assimilate.'"

**Observable markers for Level 11:**
- Several distinct supporting ideas, each developed with detail
- Precise descriptive language for shape/function/relationship
- Adapts language successfully to unusual or unfamiliar topics
- Coherent multi-point arguments

### Level 10 — Highly Effective Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Communicate in **moderately demanding** non-routine situations
- Present information and develop ideas with **clear** descriptions or reasons

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can communicate in moderately demanding non-routine situations. In Task B, the speaker finds words and phrases to describe the strange object and then makes several comparisons with more common objects (e.g., end table, desk, shelf, etc.) to describe it more accurately for the listener."

**Observable markers for Level 10:**
- Uses comparison strategies
- Multiple supporting reasons with concrete examples
- Clear development of each idea with related detail
- Slightly less precision than Level 11 but still consistently clear

### Level 9 — Effective Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Present information and support ideas with **moderately complex reasons**

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can present information clearly and support ideas with moderately complex reasons. In Task A, the speaker offers several supporting reasons why it's preferable to take the job (e.g., vacations, professional growth, applying knowledge, etc.). This is organized with the use of several linking words (e.g., and, but, so) and useful phrases that assist listeners in making logical connections (e.g., 'Though it is overseas …' and 'Looking at any of the other job offers …')."

**Observable markers for Level 9:**
- Multiple supporting reasons (typically 3-4)
- Visible use of linking words and transition phrases
- Logical connections explicitly signaled
- Reasons are developed but with less complexity than Level 10
- From Target 9+ samples: provides several main ideas, connects and develops ideas, organizes response with transitions
- This is the **threshold for Express Entry full CRS points**

### Level 8 — Good Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Present **concrete and some abstract** information that is supported with **moderately complex** reasons

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can present concrete and some abstract information and provide moderately complex reasons for their opinions. In Task A, the speaker specifies that by taking the job, the other person will gain 'professional development,' become 'stronger,' and 'learn from [their] mistakes.'"

**Observable markers for Level 8:**
- Mix of concrete details and some abstract concepts
- Reasons present but less developed than Level 9
- From Target 9+ samples: provides main ideas but doesn't develop them as much; uses basic transitions (and, but, so, also)
- Persuasive language begins to emerge with simpler sophistication

### Level 7 — Adequate Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Present **concrete and some abstract** information supported with **moderately complex** reasons

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can present concrete and some abstract information and provide moderately complex reasons for their opinions. In Task A, the speaker provides several reasons for advising the other person to take the job (e.g., it's exciting, meeting new people, getting exposed to different cultures and food, etc.)."

**Observable markers for Level 7:**
- Multiple reasons given but reasoning depth is moderate
- Concrete content dominates; abstract content is present but limited
- Recommendations are supported but with simpler justifications
- This is the **threshold for Federal Skilled Worker eligibility**

### Level 6 — Developing Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Support opinions and feelings with **reasons**
- Relate **factual information**

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can express preferences, feelings, and opinions and are able to sometimes support them with reasons. In Task A, the speaker is able to clearly express a preference for the other person to take the job for the 'betterment of [their] family.'"

**Observable markers for Level 6:**
- Opinions expressed clearly but supporting reasoning is sometimes thin
- Factual descriptions present but limited in precision
- Personal observations included but less developed
- Reasoning sometimes implied rather than stated explicitly

### Level 5 — Acquiring Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Communicate **feelings and preferences**
- Describe **common objects, situations, and people** known to the speaker
- Tell a **simple story** about something that happened

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can communicate their feelings and preferences. In Task A, the speaker provides additional reasons for taking the job by adding, 'Nowadays, there's lots of communications that we can have.'"

**Observable markers for Level 5:**
- Communication centers on feelings and preferences
- Descriptions stay at the level of "common" or familiar objects
- Stories are told but without complex structure
- Reasoning is simple and personal-experience-based

## 8. Criterion 2 — Vocabulary

**Definition:** The range, precision, and appropriateness of the speaker's word choice, including use of figures of speech and idioms.

**Guiding Question (rater-side):** *What is the range of the test taker's vocabulary and can they use it naturally?*

**Important Factors:**
- Word choice
- Suitable use of words and phrases
- Range of words and phrases
- Precision and accuracy

### Level 12

**Descriptor:**
- Use a **very broad range** of concrete and abstract language
- Use a **broad range** of figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use a broad range of concrete and abstract vocabulary and use figures of speech and idioms accurately. In Task B, the speaker describes the chair by stating, 'there's a light on the top of it which kind of winds around to focus in on where you would be sitting in the center of the chair' and later indicates that 'if you fancied a snooze as you tend to do when you're reading, you could just turn off the light and drift off.'"

**Observable markers for Level 12 vocabulary:**
- Idioms and figurative expressions used naturally and accurately
- Spatial and descriptive language is precise
- Mix of concrete and abstract vocabulary
- From Target 9+ Task 4B (Level 12): "wide range of precise, descriptive words, phrases, and expressions accurately (in terms of, I would imagine, currently, persuade, goodie, quite stern, fall out of the display)"

### Level 11

**Descriptor:**
- Use a **broad range** of concrete and abstract language
- Use a **range** of figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use a broad range of concrete and abstract vocabulary and incorporate figures of speech or idioms. In Task A, the speaker advises the other person to 'start looking into their language' and think about the challenges of assimilation."

**Observable markers for Level 11 vocabulary:**
- Idioms and figurative phrases present and accurate
- Abstract vocabulary used confidently
- Range is broad but not yet at "very broad" Level 12 territory
- Choices are consistently appropriate to context

### Level 10

**Descriptor:**
- Use a **range** of concrete and abstract language
- Use **some** figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use a range of concrete and abstract language as well as figures of speech or idioms. In Task A, the speaker indicates that the other person has already 'gone so far' in their studies and states, 'I'm sure gonna miss you.' In Task B, the speaker describes the chair as 'futuristic looking' and as having a 'half-moon shape.'"

**Observable markers for Level 10 vocabulary:**
- Figurative phrases used with informal idioms
- Descriptive vocabulary is specific and visualizable
- Some abstract vocabulary
- Range is solid but more limited than Level 11

### Level 9

**Descriptor:**
- Use **common and context-specific** vocabulary
- Use **some** figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis (from celpip.ca):**

> "Speakers can incorporate common and more context-specific vocabulary, figures of speech, and idioms into their responses. In Task A, the speaker uses 'skillset,' 'show them around,' and 'grow to great heights' to communicate with more precision."

**Rater Analysis (from Target 9+ Task 1B sample, Level 9):**

> "Uses many suitable and precise phrases (once you land there, forward your resume, afford accommodation). Word choice is imprecise at times (really good university, pretty expensive, good thing, great help)."

**Rater Analysis (from Target 9+ Task 2B sample, Level 9):**

> "Uses a range of less common words and phrases (algorithms, I have to admit, touchy subject). Uses words accurately and appropriately. Confuses one expression (touchy-feely vs. touchy) but corrects the error."

**Observable markers for Level 9 vocabulary:**
- Context-specific vocabulary appears naturally
- Idioms used but in fewer numbers than Level 10
- Word choice is precise enough to clearly communicate intended meaning
- Some imprecise word choices may exist alongside accurate ones
- Self-correction of vocabulary errors is positive (shows awareness)

### Level 8

**Descriptor:**
- Use **common words** and **some** context-specific vocabulary
- Use **some** figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis (from celpip.ca):**

> "Speakers can use common words more precisely than lower levels and incorporate some context-specific vocabulary, figures of speech, or idioms. In Task A, the speaker uses 'professional development' and 'benefits' appropriately."

**Rater Analysis (from Target 9+ Task 1A sample, Level 8):**

> "Uses suitable words and phrases (initiate, smooth, creeped out, ask out, casually). Some awkward expressions and incorrect usage (kind of a little bit wrong way, start asking for lunch, ask for a drink)."

**Observable markers for Level 8 vocabulary:**
- Context-specific terms appear but are simpler than Level 9
- Some awkward or imprecise phrasing alongside accurate vocabulary
- Less idiomatic richness than Level 9

### Level 7

**Descriptor:**
- Use **common words** and **some more precise vocabulary**
- Use **some** figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use common words and phrases to communicate meaning with more precision than speakers at lower levels and are able to use some figures of speech or idioms. In Task A, the speaker states, 'You'll establish yourself really well in the new city.' In Task B, the speaker describes the chair as 'fancy looking' and one that will 'attract attention.'"

**Observable markers for Level 7 vocabulary:**
- Common words used with reasonable precision

- Completeness

**Footnote:** Hedging that does not add task-relevant content counts as off-topic. ## 12. Task-Specific Strategies and Scored Samples

Source: CELPIP Speaking Pro: Target 9+ Study Pack (Paragon, © 2020), supplemented with prep workflows from the per-task think-aloud webinar PDFs (Speaking_Task_1.pdf, Speaking_Test_8.pdf). For each of the 8 task types, this section provides the official task description, key strategies, prep workflow (where available), and two scored sample responses with full criterion-by-criterion analysis.

### Task 1: Giving Advice

**Description:** Help someone to either make a decision or prepare for something.
**Prep Time:** 30 seconds | **Speaking Time:** 90 seconds

**Key Strategies (official):**
1. Speak directly to the person.
2. Provide at least three suggestions.
3. Give a practical example or reason for each suggestion.
4. Signal each new suggestion with a transition word or phrase such as: "If I were you,…" "Another thing you could do is…" "One final thing is…"
5. Use a helpful and thoughtful tone.

**Prep Workflow (from Speaking_Task_1.pdf think-aloud):**

The 30 seconds of prep time should produce 3 buckets matching 3 sub-tasks. For a "first day at work" prompt:

| Bucket 1 | Bucket 2 | Bucket 3 |
|---|---|---|
| Try to make a good first impression | Introduce yourself | Remember people's names |
| Dress up | (specific actions) | (specific actions) |

Each bucket becomes one main suggestion with a specific example. Transition phrases connect the buckets ("First of all…", "Next…", "Another thing you should do…", "It might be worth considering…").

**Level 9+ Technique — Fabrication with Experience Anchoring:**

When the speaker has limited direct experience with the situation in the prompt, they can fabricate plausible details and anchor them in their own experience or expertise. Example: a teacher giving advice about a software job uses general workplace experience (first impressions, dressing up, introducing yourself) and anchors it in concrete language ("Try to look people in the eye when you introduce yourself"). This is rewarded, not penalized — fabrication is an explicit Paragon-endorsed strategy.

**Closing Pattern (canonical for Task 1):**

Specific advice → reassuring close. Example: *"Well, I hope that helps you. Good luck on your first day!"* This wraps the response, signals task completion, and matches the friendly-helpful tone register.

### Task 2: Talking about a Personal Experience

**Description:** Tell a story about a past experience.
**Prep Time:** 30 seconds | **Speaking Time:** 60 seconds

**Key Strategies (official):**
1. Quickly decide what story you will tell.
2. If you can't think of a real experience from your life, make one up.
3. Use past tenses.
4. Include descriptive details.
5. Try to get your listener to feel how you felt.

**Critical sub-task structure (from Following Instructions webinar):**

Task 2 prompts almost always have **multiple parts** the speaker must address. Example: "Talk about a time when you acted as a leader. What was the activity, how did you lead others, and what was the final result?" Three sub-tasks: *what*, *how*, *result*. Skipping any of them is a Task Fulfillment hit even if the response is otherwise strong.

#### Task 2, Sample A — CELPIP Level 8

**Prompt:** Talk about a time you had to teach something to someone. Maybe you can talk about teaching a game, a skill, or a tradition. What did you teach, to whom did you teach it, and how did you teach it?

**Transcript:**
> "The one time that I had to teach someone, it was a couple of years ago when I had to teach my friend Peter to ski. Because I'm an avid skier and I have a, a long-time experience. So, he asked me to teach him. So, first I explained him the basics, how to mount the skis onto your boots, strap them down, and everything. So, obviously I didn't take him up to the highest peak. But I picked a moderate slope..."

**Analysis (Level 8):**
- **Content/Coherence:** Describes the main aspects of what he taught (e.g., how to brake, mount boots to skis). Organizes response with common transitions (and, but, so, because). More development of ideas and more specific details needed to reach Level 9+.
- **Vocabulary:** Uses a range of context-specific words and phrases (avid skier, highest peak, mount skis, moderate slope, steep). Some awkward phrases (long-time experience).
- **Listenability:** Pronunciation is clear; rhythm is fairly steady. Interjections and self-corrections occasionally interfere with flow. Some awkward/incorrect constructions (I explained him the basics, we walked it up).
- **Task Fulfillment:** Addresses all parts of the task (though greater development of ideas would enhance completeness). Speaks for the full time. Tone is appropriate.

#### Task 2, Sample B — CELPIP Level 9

**Prompt:** Talk about a class that you really enjoyed. Maybe you can talk about a class at school or college. Maybe you can talk about a fun class such as cooking or music. You decide.

**Transcript:**
> "It's been such a long time since I've taken a class, but I do, I do believe that algorithms was one of my favourite classes. Um, I guess I really enjoyed the fact that uh we were always learning something new and it was always making your brain work. Um, it was challenging and very interesting. Um honestly, I have to admit that I loved correcting the professor um with the examples and so on, which is kind of a touchy-feely, uh, touchy subject right now since I am the one teaching and the students are correcting me..."

**Analysis (Level 9):**
- **Content/Coherence:** Provides several reasons (learning something new, making her brain work). Organizes response with transitions and other linking devices (It's been such a long time, Honestly). Develops some ideas (e.g., why it's okay to correct the professor).
- **Vocabulary:** Uses a range of less common words and phrases (algorithms, I have to admit, touchy subject). Uses words accurately and appropriately. Confuses one expression (touchy-feely vs. touchy) but corrects the error.
- **Listenability:** Rhythm is steady and pronunciation is clear. Intonation adds feeling to the response. Speaker demonstrates some control over complex grammatical structures (e.g., I'm hoping my professors were taking it in the same way as I do).
- **Task Fulfillment:** Most information is relevant to the question. Response has a good opening and closing. Tone is excellent.

### Task 3: Describing a Scene

**Description:** Look at an illustration and describe what is happening.
**Prep Time:** 30 seconds | **Speaking Time:** 60 seconds

**Key Strategies (official):**
1. Start with a general statement.
2. Then focus on some details. You don't need to describe everything.
3. Build a picture.
4. Use descriptive words and phrases.
5. Describe the people's appearance, actions, and feelings.

**Organization 2 template (from Scoring/Performance Standards webinar):**

1. General statement (overview of setting)
2. Provide details (specific people, objects, actions)
3. Make connections (link people/objects to each other using prepositions)

**Tense control:** Task 3 requires *present continuous* and *present simple* describing what is currently happening ("There is a girl on the right side wearing a helmet"). Task 4 requires *future constructions* describing what will happen next. Mixing these up is a Task Fulfillment error.

#### Task 3, Sample A — CELPIP Level 9

**Prompt:** Describe some things that are happening in the picture below as well as you can. The person with whom you are speaking cannot see the picture. (Image: supermarket scene with cashiers, customers, kids, vegetables)

**Transcript:**
> "Well I can see a supermarket hall. Um, it's quite large, not very busy, there are about nine people there. I can see two cashiers. One of them is closed, so only one register is actually opened. Uh, there are three rows at the supermarket. Um, there are a lot of vegetables, uh selling uh looks like oranges, and lemons are on sale. There are two kids who are very excited. They are picking up uh the watermelons..."

**Analysis (Level 9):**
- **Content/Coherence:** Sets the scene and provides some detail (not very busy, about nine people). Provides accurate descriptions of people and objects. Response is organized well with quite a few details (only one register is opened, lemons are on sale).
- **Vocabulary:** Uses words and phrases accurately to describe people, objects, and actions (register, cashier, picking up). A greater range of more precise/complex words and phrases would further improve the response.
- **Listenability:** Rhythm is steady and pronunciation is clear. Some hesitations and self-corrections; these have a minimal impact on flow. Good control of grammatical structures.
- **Task Fulfillment:** Information is relevant. Response is quite complete although the final idea is cut off. Tone is appropriate, but the speaker doesn't address another person directly.

#### Task 3, Sample B — CELPIP Level 8

**Transcript:**

- **Listenability:** Pronunciation is clear. Pauses and self-corrections interfere with rhythm. Uses simple sentence structures well; attempts a moderately complex sentence at the end (While it's not very busy at the counter…).

### Task 4: Making Predictions

**Description:** Look at the same illustration from Task 3 and describe what you think will happen next.
**Prep Time:** 30 seconds | **Speaking Time:** 60 seconds

**Key Strategies (official):**
1. Use your imagination, but be realistic. Your predictions should be related to what you see the people doing.
2. Ask yourself: What actions might the people do next, and what feelings might they experience as a result?
3. Consider the surrounding people and objects in the picture to help you think of ideas.

**Critical addition (Scoring/Performance Standards webinar):** "Be clear which person, people, or things your prediction is about." Pronoun-only predictions hurt clarity. ### Task 5: Comparing and Persuading

**Description:** In Part 1, select an option from two choices (no speaking). In Part 2, persuade someone that your chosen option is better than the one they are proposing.
**Prep Time:** Part 1: 60 seconds | Part 2: 60 seconds
**Speaking Time:** Part 1: No speaking | Part 2: 60 seconds

**Key Strategies (official):**

*Part 1:*
1. Read and understand the situation.
2. Compare the information provided for the two options.
3. Quickly choose which one you prefer to talk about.

*Part 2:*
4. Read the information about the new option.
5. Think of details and reasons to explain why your choice is better.
6. Use specific language to explain your choice, and expand on the information included with the picture.

**Prep scaffold (Main Points of Comparison grid, from Scoring/Performance Standards webinar):**

For each comparable feature (cost, sleeping arrangement, bathrooms, kitchen, etc.), list both options' attributes side by side, then form a one-sentence persuasion point per feature. Synthesize by feature in the response, not by option:

> "First of all, when we look at cost versus value… The sleeping arrangement is much more comfortable at the B and B… We also need to consider the toilet situation… The B and B also offers the convenience of a hot breakfast."

#### Task 5, Sample A — CELPIP Level 8

**Prompt:** Choose between consignment store and food court franchise (Part 1). Then persuade your friend that your choice is better than delivery service (Part 2). Test taker chose food court franchise.

**Transcript:**
> "Well, food court franchise is much better idea than delivery service. First of all, you already know how to cook since you've been cooking food for five years now right? And um, um we both know that you have back injury and you cannot lift heavy weights and delivery service includes larger items such as furniture or TVs, so it's not a good idea for your back, like, for mine too actually. Also, to start a food franchise business we only need eight thousand dollars, and for delivery service we need forty-thousand dollars..."

**Analysis (Level 8):**
- **Content/Coherence:** Provides three main ideas (knowing how to cook, back injuries, and cost). Uses basic transitions to organize the response (and, also, but, so). More development of ideas (examples or details) would improve the content.
- **Vocabulary:** Uses some uncommon words and phrases appropriately (back injury, interact, that kind of money). A greater range of words and phrases would enable the speaker to be more precise.
- **Listenability:** Pronunciation is clear and rhythm is steady. Mostly uses simple grammar structures.
- **Task Fulfillment:** Information is relevant. Addresses the other person directly. Speaks for the full time.

#### Task 5, Sample B — CELPIP Level 9

**Prompt:** Choose between large floor fan and ceiling fan (Part 1). Then persuade your family that your choice is better than a hand-held mini fan (Part 2). Test taker chose large floor fan.

**Transcript:**
> "I agree that we have choices, but I believe the large floor fan is the most suitable for our environment because we require the fan to be installed in our basement. I agree that the ceiling fan is a good option for our living room, but since we require the fan for the basement, I would say $49.99 is an affordable option and we would not require any extra installation. The portable mini fan though just requires a battery, it's not a good option for our basement because it wouldn't cover the area..."

**Analysis (Level 9):**
- **Content/Coherence:** Provides several main ideas (needed for basement, affordable, portable). Connects and develops ideas (e.g., need a portable fan for the basement, an affordable option that doesn't require installation): coherence is strong.
- **Vocabulary:** Uses common words and phrases accurately (suitable for our environment, cover the area, moved around the house). Uses some figures of speech (I would say, I would go with).
- **Listenability:** Pronunciation is clear and rhythm is steady. Very few pauses, interjections, or self-corrections. Uses complex sentences fairly well, but more variety in sentence structure would improve this dimension.
- **Task Fulfillment:** Provides relevant suggestions and reasons. Uses time well and gives a complete response. Establishes a friendly but persuasive tone (e.g., I agree that we have choices).

### Task 6: Dealing with a Difficult Situation

**Description:** Explain a decision to a friend, family member, acquaintance, or customer.
**Prep Time:** 60 seconds | **Speaking Time:** 60 seconds

**Key Strategies (official):**
1. Speak directly to the person.
2. Be considerate but firm.
3. Speak in a respectful way.
4. Clearly outline the problem.
5. Explain your decision.
6. Provide effective reasons to support your decision.

**Softening phrases (Level 9+ tone management, from Scoring/Performance Standards webinar):**
- "I hope that you can understand that…"
- "I think we can all agree that…"
- "Let's try to look at it this way…"

**Critical Task Fulfillment requirement:** Task 6 prompts present an EITHER/OR choice. The speaker must explicitly select one option and address that party. Trying to address both parties or hedging on the choice is a Task Fulfillment failure.

#### Task 6, Sample A — CELPIP Level 8

**Prompt:** A customer wants to return a ripped shirt. Your coworker won't return it because the customer wore it. EITHER talk to your coworker (explain why it should be returned) OR talk to the customer (explain why it can't be returned). Test taker chose to talk to coworker.

**Transcript:**
> "Hello Eugene, I've just heard that you had an argument with the customer regarding their ripped t-shirts. I think we have to return this t-shirts because, because, as uh as per customer she just wore only one times and it is, I guess, it clearly says that is, ah, poor quality. Well, uh, well and then and before the other time I remember that the same thing happened with another customer, and there was uh my supervisor and then the supervisor said we can, we can refund, refund the item when it, it clearly lacks some quality..."

**Analysis (Level 8):**
- **Content/Coherence:** Provides three main reasons to support her choice (poor quality, previous experience, possible warranty). Ideas are organized with simple linking words (because, and, so).
- **Vocabulary:** Uses common words and phrases appropriate to the situation (quality, brand, double check). A greater range of vocabulary and phrase use would improve precision and accuracy.
- **Listenability:** Pronunciation is clear and rhythm is fairly steady. Interjections and self-corrections sometimes interfere with flow. Uses a variety of sentence structures, but makes some grammatical errors (this t-shirts, only one times, as based on).
- **Task Fulfillment:** Relevant information. Appropriate tone. Speaks for the full time. Saying "Thank you" at the end is awkward for this context.

#### Task 6, Sample B — CELPIP Level 9

**Prompt:** A couple comes to a pet store. Wife wants a dog (friendly, playful). Husband wants a cat (independent, easier to care for). EITHER suggest a cat OR suggest a dog. Test taker agreed with the husband (cat).

**Transcript:**
> "I strongly agree with the husband because I believe buying a cat is much easier for the couple to take, to for them, it's much easier for them to take care of the, the pet because the, uh of course uh the cat is smaller and uh it need uh less care than the, the dog. **Analysis (Level 9):**

### Task 7: Expressing Opinions

**Description:** Answer a "Do you think…?" question with your opinion.
**Prep Time:** 30 seconds | **Speaking Time:** 90 seconds

**Key Strategies (official):**
1. Read the question carefully; be sure your response is on topic.
2. Quickly decide whether you will answer yes or no.
3. Give about three reasons to support your position.
4. Support each reason with specific details.
5. Present your ideas logically, and use transitions to signal new ideas.

**Prep scaffold (from Scoring/Performance Standards webinar) — Main Idea / Supporting Details two-column outline:**

| Main Idea | Supporting Detail |
|---|---|
| Reason 1 | One concrete example |
| Reason 2 | One concrete example |
| Reason 3 | One concrete example |

90 seconds of speaking time accommodates 3 main ideas with one developed example each. Trying to fit more reasons in usually means none get developed.

#### Task 7, Sample A — CELPIP Level 8

**Prompt:** Do you think that governments should limit how much money company bosses make? Explain your reasons.

**Transcript:**
> "This is a very difficult question, uh I do think that government should limit how much money company bosses make for at least two main reasons. The first one is ethical. If uh you provide uh bosses no limit in their earning, this will raise significant [unclear – likely social] disparities and will create more friction among the society. However, the limit that the government should put on the earning is not easy to determine. Competition, willingness to improve our position and career, is often uh moved by money and earning and salary..."

**Analysis (Level 8):**
- **Content/Coherence:** Contradicts himself: starts off arguing for the "yes" side, gives one reason to support it (ethics), but then seems to switch to the "no" side (competition, willingness to improve our position). Uses the transition phrase "The first one is," but introduces no further reasons.
- **Vocabulary:** Accurately uses a range of words and phrases throughout the response (ethical, disparities, friction, position, career, cutting salaries). Some imprecise and unnatural word and phrase use (provide no limit, among the society).
- **Listenability:** Pronunciation creates occasional challenges for the listener. Reducing pauses would improve rhythm. Uses a variety of sentence structures, but some constructions are awkward (not so significant than someone else's limit).
- **Task Fulfillment:** Spends more time describing the problems his choice would cause than giving reasons why it's the best choice. Tone is appropriate.

**Note for AI scoring:** This sample is instructive — strong vocabulary and complex grammar can still produce a Level 8 (not Level 9) score if Content/Coherence is weakened by self-contradiction. *All four criteria matter independently.*

#### Task 7, Sample B — CELPIP Level 9

**Prompt:** Do you think all high school students should be required to learn a second language? Explain your reasons.

**Transcript:**
> "Yes, I think it's very beneficial for high school students to be required to learn a second language, especially here in Canada to learn French. I've seen it in the workplace, it's very beneficial for, um, for workers to have a second language, especially French. Um because uh first thing, more compensation if you're bilingual, French bilingual, right, that's first thing for me. And of course it opens up a whole new like field of work where you, if you're not bilingual or you don't have a second language, you will never be able to enter..."

**Analysis (Level 9):**
- **Content/Coherence:** Gives three clear reasons (compensation, work opportunities and travel abroad). Develops some of the reasons with supporting details or examples. Organizes ideas with common linking words (first thing, so, also, but).
- **Vocabulary:** Uses a range of common words and phrases accurately (bilingual, field of work, fluent). Uses some expressions appropriately (opens up, learns the basics). More precision and variety would strengthen the response.
- **Listenability:** Pronunciation is clear and rhythm is fairly steady. Self-corrections have a minor impact on flow. Uses some complex sentence structure (e.g., if clauses), but could include more variety.
- **Task Fulfillment:** Information is relevant. Tone is appropriate. Speaks for the full time. Has a good opening; lacks a closing.

### Task 8: Describing an Unusual Situation

**Description:** Describe a picture of an unusual item or situation to someone who can't see what you're looking at.
**Prep Time:** 30 seconds | **Speaking Time:** 60 seconds

**Key Strategies (official):**
1. Start by setting up the role-play scenario ("Hi, Jill…")
2. Describe the unique aspects of the picture.
3. Focus on the things that you can describe well.
4. If you can't think of accurate and specific vocabulary, create phrases using common words to express your meaning.
5. End by finishing the phone call.

**Prep Workflow (from Speaking_Test_8.pdf think-aloud) — 5W mind map:**

Build the response around four W-questions with the prompt's keyword bank as the center:

| Who | What | Where | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| (e.g., uncle, excellent soccer player) | (e.g., half-time show, mascots playing a game, charity) | (e.g., soccer stadium, Western University) | (e.g., volunteering his time, representing his university) |

This generates 4 buckets of content from a 30-second prep window.

**Sub-task structure:** Task 8 prompts have TWO explicit sub-tasks signaled by on-screen banners:
1. **Describe** the unusual scene/object
2. **Ask/invite** the listener (whether they want to come, would buy it, would join the game, etc.)

The "ask/invite" component is non-negotiable. Describe-only responses lose Task Fulfillment points even if description is excellent.

**Level 9+ Techniques (canonical for Task 8):**

1. **Descriptive vocabulary** — "hilarious hot dog costume," "supposed to be relish," "dominating the play"
2. **Complex sentences using "which"** — "He's wearing a hilarious hot dog costume and a green wig, **which** I think is supposed to be relish"
3. **Background experience + imagination** — invent context the picture doesn't show ("your brother," "Western University," "for charity") to make the description richer
4. **Varied sentence structure** — interleave conditionals, relative clauses, and simple statements

**Negative anchor (low band):** Three choppy simple sentences = low score. *"He's wearing a wig. It's green. It looks like relish."* The Level 9+ rewrite uses subordination: *"He's wearing a green wig, which I think is supposed to be relish."*

#### Task 8, Sample A — CELPIP Level 8

**Prompt:** A new friend invited you to visit his home. You think you've found the street where he lives. However, you cannot find his house. Call your friend. Explain in detail where you are standing. Ask him how to find his house.

**Transcript:**
> "Here we go, how are you? Here, I'm exactly near to your house, but I'm really not sure where I need to move. It's on the right si–, I have to take the right street or the left street. I'll just explain you a little bit so you have an idea where exactly I am. Um, I can see a board on which it's written S-L-O-W, it's a yellow board with a black uh [unclear] written on it, and there's one garbage bin near to it and uh its open..."

**Analysis (Level 8):**
- **Content/Coherence:** States the reason for the call. Provides basic description of some objects in the scene. Uses simple linking words (and, but, so).
- **Vocabulary:** Uses common words accurately (garbage bin, townhouse). Uses some inaccurate and imprecise phrases (right or left street, I'll explain you, down the townhouse).
- **Listenability:** Pronunciation is usually clear and rhythm is fairly steady. Interjections and self-corrections occasionally disrupt flow. Uses mostly simple sentence constructions; most grammatical errors don't interfere with meaning.
- **Task Fulfillment:** Some details are unnecessary (e.g., the garbage bin is open). Tone is appropriate (friendly, confused, and seeking help). No closing.

#### Task 8, Sample B — CELPIP Level 9

**Prompt:** You are at a tourist site and see some street performers. Call your friend Katia and describe in detail what the performers are doing, and ask her if she would like to see the event.

**Transcript:**
> "Hi Katia, do you hear this uh music behind me? Uh, I'm in a park somewhere downtown, I don't know basically, and uh these uh street performers are actually having a really good band. And the instruments are of course glasses filled to different volumes and uh a percussion set made of garbage cans, or you know buckets, plastic ones and metal ones, and it actually, you wouldn't believe it, sounds very good..."

**Analysis (Level 9):**
- **Content/Coherence:** Describes several objects with some precision. Organizes ideas by using linking words and giving additional context.
- **Vocabulary:** Uses a range of situation-specific words and phrases (percussion set, soundtrack, they've been going at it for hours, you wouldn't believe it, check them out). Some inaccurate expressions (having a good band).
- **Listenability:** Pronunciation is usually clear; rhythm and intonation are used effectively. Uses a range of sentence structures.
- **Task Fulfillment:** Gives relevant information and uses the time effectively. Tone is very friendly and engaging. Speaks directly to the person.

## 13. Per-Task Task Fulfillment Checklists

Source: Following Instructions webinar (Paragon). Task Fulfillment is task-specific. The AI scorer must verify each item per task type. Failure on any item is a Task Fulfillment hit.

### Task 1: Giving Advice
- [ ] Speaks **directly** to the person (uses "you," addresses by name if given)
- [ ] Provides **at least 3 distinct suggestions**
- [ ] Each suggestion is supported by a reason or example
- [ ] Uses transitions to signal new suggestions
- [ ] Tone is helpful and friendly
- [ ] Includes a closing (encouragement is canonical: "Good luck!", "I hope that helps!")

### Task 2: Talking about a Personal Experience
- [ ] Uses **past tense** consistently
- [ ] Addresses **all sub-tasks** in the prompt (most prompts have 2-3 explicit questions: who/what/where/how/result)
- [ ] Includes descriptive details that help the listener visualize
- [ ] Communicates how the speaker felt
- [ ] Has a clear opening (sets up the story)
- [ ] Has a clear closing (resolves the story)

### Task 3: Describing a Scene
- [ ] Uses **present continuous and present simple** (NOT future tense — that's Task 4)
- [ ] Starts with a general statement establishing the setting
- [ ] Describes specific people, objects, and actions
- [ ] Makes connections between elements (using prepositions: next to, behind, in front of)
- [ ] Optional: addresses an absent listener (phone-call frame is encouraged in some prompts)
- [ ] Uses descriptive vocabulary, not generic ("the man in the green sweater," not "that guy")

### Task 4: Making Predictions
- [ ] Uses **future tense** ("will," "is going to," "probably will," "is likely to")
- [ ] Predictions are **realistic** given what the picture shows
- [ ] Predictions are **clear about who/what** they refer to (subject named explicitly, not pronoun-only)
- [ ] Multiple predictions covering different parts of the picture
- [ ] Has a strategy/structure (works through people one at a time, or by sub-scene)

### Task 5: Comparing and Persuading
- [ ] **States preference** clearly at the start
- [ ] Compares on **multiple features** (cost, suitability, comfort, etc.)
- [ ] Uses persuasive language addressed to the listener
- [ ] Acknowledges the other option (often "I agree that…" then redirect)
- [ ] Friendly but persuasive tone
- [ ] Speaks for full time

### Task 6: Dealing with a Difficult Situation
- [ ] **Explicitly selects** one of the two options (Either Person A or Person B)
- [ ] Addresses the chosen person **directly**
- [ ] Clearly outlines the problem
- [ ] Explains the decision
- [ ] Provides reasons to support the decision
- [ ] Uses considerate-but-firm tone (softening phrases for tone management)
- [ ] Does NOT try to address both parties

### Task 7: Expressing Opinions
- [ ] Takes a **clear position** (Yes/No/Agree/Disagree) at the start
- [ ] Maintains the same position throughout (no self-contradiction)
- [ ] Provides **about 3 reasons** supporting the position
- [ ] Supports each reason with a specific detail or example
- [ ] Uses transitions between reasons (First of all, In addition, Also, Finally)
- [ ] Uses the full 90 seconds

### Task 8: Describing an Unusual Situation
- [ ] **Both sub-tasks addressed**: (1) describe, (2) ask/invite
- [ ] Sets up the role-play frame (e.g., "Hi Dad!", "Hey Katia!")
- [ ] Description is precise enough that the listener can imagine the scene
- [ ] Describes unique/unusual aspects (not generic)
- [ ] Tone matches the prompt (excited if unusual, helpful if seeking help)
- [ ] Ends the phone call appropriately
- [ ] Leaves enough time to complete BOTH parts

## 14. Vocabulary Upgrade Techniques (Level 8 → 9+)

Source: Vocabulary webinar (Paragon). These are the four explicit techniques Paragon teaches for moving from Level 8 to Level 9+. The AI's "How to Improve" feedback should reference these techniques by name when vocabulary is the limiting factor.

### Technique 1: Paraphrase the Prompt

Don't echo the prompt's wording. Use synonyms and paraphrases to demonstrate range.

**Before (echoes prompt):** *"My friend was hired for a position at a software development company..."*
**After (paraphrases):** *"My friend just landed her first job at a tech company..."*

### Technique 2: Vary Modals

Replace flat verb phrases with modal-based variation: *should, could, might, ought to, would, will need to, may want to.*

**Before:** *"You should make a good impression. You should dress nicely. You should be on time."*
**After:** *"You'll want to make a good first impression. It might also help to dress smartly. And of course, you ought to arrive a few minutes early."*

### Technique 3: Replace Generic Adjectives

Replace *good, bad, nice, big, small, important* with more precise alternatives.

**Before:** *"It's a good idea. It's a big company."*
**After:** *"It's a smart move. It's a well-established firm."*

### Technique 4: Combine Choppy Sentences

Use subordinating conjunctions, relative clauses ("which," "who," "that"), and participial phrases to combine short sentences into complex ones.

**Before:** *"He's wearing a wig. It's green. It looks like relish."*
**After:** *"He's wearing a green wig, which I think is supposed to be relish."*

### Cross-Anchor: The Mall/Phone Before/After Pattern

Paragon's gold-standard feedback format is the **before/after pair** — show the speaker's actual phrase, then the upgraded version. The AI feedback in v1 should follow this format whenever possible:

**Speaker said:** *"I went to the mall to buy a phone."*
**Upgraded:** *"I headed to the mall to pick up a new smartphone."*

Each upgrade should isolate ONE technique so the user can apply it. Stacking 4 techniques in one rewrite is overwhelming — it's better to provide 2-3 separate before/after pairs each demonstrating one technique.

## 15. Listenability — Pause and Self-Correction Taxonomy

Source: Performance Standards webinar (Paragon). Listenability is the most contested criterion. This taxonomy clarifies what counts as helpful vs. harmful in the "Pauses, interjections, and self-correction" Important Factor.

### Pause Taxonomy

| Pause Type | Description | Effect on Score |
|---|---|---|
| **Appropriate pause** | Brief pause at clause boundary, after punctuation-equivalent in speech, or for emphasis | **Neutral or positive** — natural rhythm |
| **Random pause** | Mid-clause, mid-phrase, mid-word | **Negative** — interferes with rhythm |
| **Long pause (3+ seconds)** | Extended silence regardless of position | **Strongly negative** — fluency breakdown |

### Interjection List (full Paragon list)

The following are considered interjections that affect Listenability when frequent:
- *uh, um, eh, hmm, err, ah*

A few interjections per response is normal at any band. Frequent interjections (one every few seconds) push toward Level 8 and below.

### Self-Correction Calibration

Self-correction is **NOT automatically a negative**. Paragon distinguishes:

- **Quick self-correction** (immediate fix, 1-2 words): **Positive** — shows monitoring and accuracy awareness. The Target 9+ Level 9 sample explicitly praises this: *"Confuses one expression (touchy-feely vs. touchy) but corrects the error."*
- **Frequent self-correction** (multiple per sentence, struggling to land on a word): **Negative** — interferes with fluency
- **Extended self-correction** (restart entire clauses, abandon and restart sentences): **Strongly negative** — flags weak grammar/vocabulary control

### AI Scoring Implication

For text-only scoring of Listenability, the AI should:

1. Count interjections (uh/um/eh/hmm/err/ah) per 60 seconds. Heuristic: 0-3 = Level 9+, 4-7 = Level 7-8, 8+ = Level 6 or below.
2. Detect self-correction markers in transcript (false starts, repeated words, "I mean," "or rather"). Quick corrections are NOT penalized; only frequent/extended corrections.
3. Use Whisper word timestamps (when available) to flag long pauses (3+ seconds mid-utterance).
4. Analyze sentence complexity from text directly: subordinating conjunctions, relative clauses, conditionals = positive; only simple sentences strung with "and/but" = negative.

The AI's Listenability feedback should explicitly disclose what was scored from text vs. inferred from audio markers (per the v1 transparency requirement).

## 16. Level 9 Performance Profile

This is the official Paragon-published profile for CELPIP Speaking Level 9 — the most common target for Express Entry candidates. Source: Speaking Pro: Target 9+ Study Pack.

### Level 9 Self-Description ("At this level, I can…")

| Content/Coherence | Vocabulary | Listenability | Task Fulfillment | When |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Present information and support ideas with moderately complex reasons | Use common or context-specific vocabulary; use some figures of speech and idioms | Speak with some control of complex grammatical structures; speak intelligibly with mostly fluent rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation | Adjust my speaking style and tone to a range of audiences and situations; convey my ideas to others when I speak | I speak with peers or authority figures; face-to-face, online, or on the phone; in formal or informal situations |

### Level 9 Performance Profile (verbatim)

> "I can communicate complex details, feelings, and attitudes in familiar and less familiar contexts. I have control of some complex grammar, and an adequate professional repertoire of precise and context-specific vocabulary. My mistakes in grammar, word choice, and pronunciation rarely make it hard for people to understand my meaning."

### Level 9 "Things to Work On" (Improvement Targets)

For users who score at or below Level 9 and want to improve, Paragon's official guidance is:

1. **Use a variety of complex grammatical structures.**
2. **Increase the ability to use context-specific vocabulary.**
3. **Reduce the number of grammar, word choice, and pronunciation errors.**
4. **Speak in a tone and style appropriate to the situation and audience.**

### Implications for the Product

This is the core foundation for the **"How to Improve" feature** in the AI scoring tool:

- When the AI scores a response at Level 8 or below, the feedback should reference these four improvement targets directly
- When the AI scores a response at Level 9, it should highlight which target(s) the response is closest to achieving at Level 10 (more complex grammar variety, more context-specific vocabulary, fewer errors, better tone match)
- The feature can be marketed as "Get to CLB 9 — the level that unlocks full Express Entry CRS points" with these four explicit improvement areas as the structure
- Vocabulary feedback should reference the four upgrade techniques (Section 14) by name
- Listenability feedback should reference the pause/self-correction taxonomy (Section 15) by name

## 17. Official Speaking Study Tips

These are the eight tips published by Paragon Testing on the CELPIP Free Resources page. They represent CELPIP's own guidance on what graders look for and what test-takers should prioritize.

1. **Take the time to practice speaking English.** Practice with friends, family, and co-workers. Remember the test is delivered through a computer with no human examiner — practice speaking into a recording device to build confidence.

2. **Follow the instructions and address the prompt.** Read all instructions before beginning. Use preparation time to read and understand the prompt and organize thoughts before speaking.

3. **Give complete answers.** Provide CELPIP raters with as much content as possible so they can accurately assess oral proficiency. Elaborate with relevant details. Do not go off topic, but speak as much as possible in the time allotted.

4. **Draw from your own life experiences.** Talking about something personal and meaningful is much easier than making something up.

5. **Speak clearly and at a natural pace.** Use the recording device test before the exam to find the best volume, tone, and pace.

6. **Try not to repeat words.** Use your own words to show full ability rather than repeating words from the prompt. Use synonyms and paraphrases to demonstrate vocabulary range.

7. **Don't worry about your accent.** Unless the accent prevents understanding, do not focus on it. Concentrate instead on good grammar, accurate and varied vocabulary, and thoroughly responding to prompts.

8. **Manage your time.** Use preparation time to understand the prompt, brainstorm ideas, and organize what to say. Use the speaking timer to ensure complete responses and finish the last sentence before time is up.

### Implications for AI Scoring

These tips reveal what CELPIP itself emphasizes in its rater training:

- **Accent is explicitly de-prioritized** — graders do not penalize for accent unless intelligibility is affected
- **Vocabulary range is rewarded** — repetition of prompt words is implicitly penalized
- **Task adherence matters** — going off-topic is a clear negative
- **Complete utilization of time** is encouraged but quality matters more than length
- **Personal/authentic content is encouraged** — strained, formulaic responses score lower than genuine ones
- **From Target 9+ Study Pack:** "make one up" is acceptable for Task 2 — fabricated stories don't lower scores if they are well-told (this is reinforced by the Task 1 fabrication-with-anchoring technique in Section 12)
