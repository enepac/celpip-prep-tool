## 2. Test Structure and Context

### CELPIP-General Test Overview

CELPIP-General is a computer-delivered English proficiency test designed by Paragon Testing Enterprises (a subsidiary of UBC). It is one of two designated English language tests for Canadian permanent residency (Express Entry and other immigration streams) and Canadian citizenship.

The test consists of four components delivered in a single 3-hour sitting:

| Component | Duration | Tasks |
|---|---|---|
| Listening | 47–55 min | 6 parts, 38 questions + 1 unscored practice |
| Reading | 55–60 min | 4 parts, 38 questions + 1 unscored practice |
| Writing | 53–60 min | 2 tasks (email + survey response) |
| **Speaking** | **15–20 min** | **8 scored tasks + 1 unscored practice** |

### Speaking Component Specifics

- All 8 Speaking tasks are scored
- One unscored practice task precedes the scored tasks
- Each task involves an on-screen prompt with preparation time, followed by a timed recorded response
- The test is delivered entirely by computer — no human examiner is present during recording
- Responses are evaluated by trained CELPIP raters using the four-criterion rubric described in this document

### Scoring and Acceptance

- CELPIP scores are accepted by **Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC)** for permanent residency and citizenship applications
- Designated by **the Australian Government's Department of Home Affairs (DHA)** for visa purposes
- Accepted for professional designations in Canada
- Accepted for the **Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP)**
- Online results in 3-4 business days
- Test fee: $290 + taxes (CELPIP-General); $195 + taxes (CELPIP-General LS, citizenship-only)

## 3. Scoring Scale and CLB/CEFR Mapping

Each Speaking response is scored on the four criteria independently. The composite Speaking score is reported on the CELPIP scale (0-12), which is calibrated to the Canadian Language Benchmark (CLB) levels.

### Full Scoring Table

| CELPIP Level | CLB Level | CEFR | Descriptor |
|---|---|---|---|
| 12 | 12 | C2 | Expert proficiency in high-stakes social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 11 | 11 | C1 | Advanced proficiency in high-stakes social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 10 | 10 | C1 | Highly effective proficiency in some high-stakes social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 9 | 9 | B2 | Effective proficiency in some high-stakes social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 8 | 8 | B2 | Good proficiency in more demanding social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 7 | 7 | B2 | Adequate proficiency in somewhat demanding social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 6 | 6 | B1 | Developing proficiency in everyday social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 5 | 5 | B1 | Acquiring proficiency in everyday social, educational, or workplace contexts |
| 4 | 4 | A2 | Adequate proficiency for daily life activities |
| 3 | 3 | A2 | Some proficiency in limited contexts of personal relevance |
| 2 | 1, 2 | A1 | Limited ability in contexts related to immediate needs |
| 1 | / | / | Insufficient information to assess |
| 0 | / | / | Insufficient information to assess |
| NA | / | / | Not Administered |

### Why CLB 7, 9, and 10 Matter Most

For Canadian immigration purposes (Express Entry — the primary use case for CELPIP candidates):

- **CLB 7** — Threshold for Federal Skilled Worker eligibility; minimum for many PR streams
- **CLB 9** — Triggers full Express Entry CRS points for language; the most common target
- **CLB 10** — Maximum Express Entry CRS points for language ability (along with CLB 11+)

This document covers Levels 5-12 because that is the working zone for the target user population. Levels below 5 are out of scope for v1 — users at those levels need foundational ESL instruction, not test prep refinement.

## 4. The Eight Speaking Tasks

The CELPIP Speaking section comprises 8 distinct task types, each requiring a different communicative function:

| # | Task Name | Communicative Function |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Giving Advice | Recommend a course of action to a friend/acquaintance based on a described situation |
| 2 | Talking about a Personal Experience | Narrate a specific past event from the speaker's life |
| 3 | Describing a Scene | Describe a picture or visual scene in detail |
| 4 | Making Predictions | Predict what will happen next in a depicted situation |
| 5 | Comparing and Persuading | Compare two options and persuade the listener toward one |
| 6 | Dealing with a Difficult Situation | Navigate an interpersonal or logistical problem (e.g., complaint, refusal, negotiation) |
| 7 | Expressing Opinions | State and defend an opinion on a topic |
| 8 | Describing an Unusual Situation | Describe an unfamiliar or unexpected scene/object to a listener who cannot see it |

### Task-Agnostic Note on Rubric Application

The four scoring criteria (Content/Coherence, Vocabulary, Listenability, Task Fulfillment) apply across all 8 task types. A CLB 9 vocabulary range looks the same in Task 1 (Giving Advice) as in Task 7 (Expressing Opinions). What changes between tasks is **the communicative situation** — what counts as fulfilling the task depends on what the task asked for.

For Task Fulfillment specifically, the rubric is applied through the lens of the task type:
- Task 1: Did the speaker provide advice that addressed the situation?
- Task 3: Did the speaker describe the scene with enough detail for a listener to picture it?
- Task 6: Did the speaker handle the difficult situation appropriately for the relationship?

## 5. The Two Reference Tasks

Paragon's official Score Comparison Chart provides scored sample responses for **only two task types**: Task A (Giving Advice) and Task B (Describing a Scene). These two tasks are used as references for all 8 levels (CLB 5-12). The transcripts and analyses in Sections 6-10 of this document refer to these two reference tasks.

### Task A — Giving Advice (Reference Prompt)

> Your friend has just graduated with a business degree and has been offered a job overseas. Although the job would be an excellent opportunity, she is not sure about moving so far away from her friends and family. Give her advice on what she should consider when making her decision.

**Format characteristics:**
- ~1:30 response duration
- Speaker addresses an absent friend (named "Joanne" or "Julie" in different samples)
- Requires balancing personal/emotional content with reasoning
- Tests ability to weigh tradeoffs and recommend action

### Task B — Describing a Scene (Reference Prompt)

> You saw an interesting chair at a furniture fair. Your friend, Maddie, collects interesting furniture. Call Maddie and describe in detail what this chair looks like, and ask her if she would consider buying it for her home office.

**Format characteristics:**
- ~1:00-1:02 response duration
- Speaker addresses an absent friend ("Maddie") who cannot see the object
- Visual prompt: image of an unusual blue curved/swing-shaped chair with attached lamp, side table, and cup holder
- Requires precise descriptive vocabulary, spatial language, and ability to convey shape/color/function to a listener without visual reference

## 6. Criterion 1 — Content/Coherence

**Definition:** The speaker's ability to organize ideas logically, support claims with reasoning, and present information with appropriate complexity for the situation.

**What raters evaluate:**
- Clarity and logical flow of ideas
- Use of linking words and transitions
- Quality and complexity of supporting reasons
- Range of communicative purposes addressed
- Ability to handle non-routine or demanding situations

### Level 12 — Expert Proficiency

**Descriptor (what speakers can do):**
- Communicate in **demanding non-routine** situations for a **full range of purposes**, intentions, and objectives
- Present information and develop ideas with **complex, clear, and precise** descriptions or details

**Rater Analysis (from Task A and Task B samples):**

> "Speakers can communicate in demanding non-routine situations for a full range of purposes, intentions, and objectives. In Task A, the speaker lessens the certainty of some statements (e.g., 'You *probably* would not be able to get a salary like this here in Canada') and strengthens other statements to indicate more certainty (e.g., 'So, you would *always* be able to come and visit your family')."

> "Speakers are also able to present information and develop ideas with complex, clear, and precise descriptions or details. In Task B, the speaker provides clear and precise details when stating, 'I should note that on the back of the chair, there's a switch to control the reading light above your head.'"

**Observable markers for AI scoring at Level 12:**
- Modulates certainty with precision (e.g., distinguishing "probably," "always," "definitely" by context)
- Provides specific, layered descriptions (not generic)
- Addresses multiple communicative purposes within a single response
- Ideas connect with logical sophistication (not just sequential listing)

### Level 11 — Advanced Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Communicate in demanding **non-routine** situations for a **range of purposes**
- Present information and develop ideas with **clear and precise** descriptions or details

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can communicate effectively in demanding non-routine situations for a range of purposes. In Task B, the speaker performs successfully in this unusual situation by selecting words and phrases to describe the strange object and explaining how a picture of it will be sent to the listener."

> "Speakers can present information and develop ideas with precise descriptions or details. In Task A, the speaker provides several ideas and develops them with precise details such as the need to research the culture to 'see if you would be able to assimilate.' In Task B, the speaker provides a precise description of the shape of the chair by precisely indicating how parts are connected."

**Observable markers for Level 11:**
- Several distinct supporting ideas, each developed with detail
- Precise descriptive language for shape/function/relationship
- Adapts language successfully to unusual or unfamiliar topics
- Coherent multi-point arguments

### Level 10 — Highly Effective Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Communicate in **moderately demanding** non-routine situations
- Present information and develop ideas with **clear** descriptions or reasons

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can communicate in moderately demanding non-routine situations. In Task B, the speaker finds words and phrases to describe the strange object and then makes several comparisons with more common objects (e.g., end table, desk, shelf, etc.) to describe it more accurately for the listener."

> "Speakers can present information and develop ideas with clear descriptions or reasons. In Task A, the speaker provides several ideas to support the opinion expressed (e.g., learn more, improve skills, and explore the world) and develops these ideas with related details and examples."

**Observable markers for Level 10:**
- Uses comparison strategies (e.g., comparing unusual object to familiar ones)
- Multiple supporting reasons with concrete examples
- Clear development of each idea with related detail
- Slightly less precision than Level 11 but still consistently clear

### Level 9 — Effective Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Present information and support ideas with **moderately complex reasons**

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can present information clearly and support ideas with moderately complex reasons. In Task A, the speaker offers several supporting reasons why it's preferable to take the job (e.g., vacations, professional growth, applying knowledge, etc.). This is organized with the use of several linking words (e.g., and, but, so) and useful phrases that assist listeners in making logical connections (e.g., 'Though it is overseas …' and 'Looking at any of the other job offers …')."

**Observable markers for Level 9:**
- Multiple supporting reasons (typically 3-4)
- Visible use of linking words and transition phrases
- Logical connections explicitly signaled
- Reasons are developed but with less complexity than Level 10
- This is the **threshold for Express Entry full CRS points** — a critical band

### Level 8 — Good Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Present **concrete and some abstract** information that is supported with **moderately complex** reasons

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can present concrete and some abstract information and provide moderately complex reasons for their opinions. In Task A, the speaker specifies that by taking the job, the other person will gain 'professional development,' become 'stronger,' and 'learn from [their] mistakes.' The speaker also persuades the other person to take the job by indicating, 'the payment is really good,' there are 'a lot of benefits,' and that the company is 'well-known.'"

**Observable markers for Level 8:**
- Mix of concrete details and some abstract concepts
- Reasons present but less developed than Level 9
- Persuasive language begins to emerge but with less sophistication
- Ideas are clear but supported with simpler reasoning

### Level 7 — Adequate Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Present **concrete and some abstract** information that is supported with **moderately complex** reasons (similar wording to Level 8 but with weaker development)

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can present concrete and some abstract information and provide moderately complex reasons for their opinions. In Task A, the speaker provides several reasons for advising the other person to take the job (e.g., it's exciting, meeting new people, getting exposed to different cultures and food, etc.). In Task B, the speaker supports the recommendation to buy the chair by stating, 'it's gonna attract additional attention to your drawing room.'"

**Observable markers for Level 7:**
- Multiple reasons given but reasoning depth is moderate
- Concrete content dominates; abstract content is present but limited
- Recommendations are supported but with simpler justifications
- This is the **threshold for Federal Skilled Worker eligibility** — a critical band

### Level 6 — Developing Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Support opinions and feelings with **reasons**
- Relate **factual information**

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can express preferences, feelings, and opinions and are able to sometimes support them with reasons. In Task A, the speaker is able to clearly express a preference for the other person to take the job for the 'betterment of [their] family.'"

> "Speakers can also relate factual information or events with some precision in most familiar contexts. In Task B, the speaker describes several details about the unusual chair and provides a personal observation when stating, 'This structure is, the design is so interesting, like no one ever had.'"

**Observable markers for Level 6:**
- Opinions expressed clearly but supporting reasoning is sometimes thin
- Factual descriptions present but limited in precision
- Personal observations included but less developed
- Reasoning sometimes implied rather than stated explicitly

### Level 5 — Acquiring Proficiency

**Descriptor:**
- Communicate **feelings and preferences**
- Describe **common objects, situations, and people** known to the speaker
- Tell a **simple story** about something that happened

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can communicate their feelings and preferences. In Task A, the speaker provides additional reasons for taking the job by adding, 'Nowadays, there's lots of communications that we can have.'"

> "Speakers can describe common objects, situations, and people that they know. In Task B, the speaker says, 'It would fit to you because uh it's very futuristic design.'"

> "Speakers can tell a story about something that happened to them. In Task A, the speaker draws on background experience when stating, 'I can say that leaving our family, it's le … to find our job, it's very hard.'"

**Observable markers for Level 5:**
- Communication centers on feelings and preferences
- Descriptions stay at the level of "common" or familiar objects
- Stories are told but without complex structure
- Reasoning is simple and personal-experience-based

## 7. Criterion 2 — Vocabulary

**Definition:** The range, precision, and appropriateness of the speaker's word choice, including use of figures of speech and idioms.

**What raters evaluate:**
- Vocabulary range (concrete vs. abstract; common vs. specific)
- Precision and accuracy of word choice
- Use of figures of speech and idioms
- Vocabulary appropriate to context and audience

### Level 12

**Descriptor:**
- Use a **very broad range** of concrete and abstract language
- Use a **broad range** of figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use a broad range of concrete and abstract vocabulary and use figures of speech and idioms accurately. In Task B, the speaker describes the chair by stating, 'there's a light on the top of it which kind of winds around to focus in on where you would be sitting in the center of the chair' and later indicates that 'if you fancied a snooze as you tend to do when you're reading, you could just turn off the light and drift off.'"

**Observable markers for Level 12 vocabulary:**
- Idioms and figurative expressions used naturally and accurately ("fancied a snooze," "drift off")
- Spatial and descriptive language is precise (e.g., "winds around to focus in on")
- Mix of concrete and abstract vocabulary
- Vocabulary range is noticeably broader than Level 11

### Level 11

**Descriptor:**
- Use a **broad range** of concrete and abstract language
- Use a **range** of figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use a broad range of concrete and abstract vocabulary and incorporate figures of speech or idioms. In Task A, the speaker advises the other person to 'start looking into their language' and think about the challenges of assimilation."

**Observable markers for Level 11 vocabulary:**
- Idioms and figurative phrases present and accurate ("start looking into," "challenges of assimilation")
- Abstract vocabulary used confidently
- Range is broad but not yet at "very broad" Level 12 territory
- Choices are consistently appropriate to context

### Level 10

**Descriptor:**
- Use a **range** of concrete and abstract language
- Use **some** figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use a range of concrete and abstract language as well as figures of speech or idioms. In Task A, the speaker indicates that the other person has already 'gone so far' in their studies and states, 'I'm sure gonna miss you.' In Task B, the speaker describes the chair as 'futuristic looking' and as having a 'half-moon shape.'"

**Observable markers for Level 10 vocabulary:**
- Figurative phrases used ("gone so far") with informal idioms ("gonna")
- Descriptive vocabulary is specific and visualizable ("futuristic looking," "half-moon shape")
- Some abstract vocabulary
- Range is solid but more limited than Level 11

### Level 9

**Descriptor:**
- Use **common and context-specific** vocabulary
- Use **some** figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can incorporate common and more context-specific vocabulary, figures of speech, and idioms into their responses. In Task A, the speaker uses 'skillset,' 'show them around,' and 'grow to great heights' to communicate with more precision. In Task B, the speaker uses 'spellbound' and 'first thought that came to my mind.'"

**Observable markers for Level 9 vocabulary:**
- Context-specific vocabulary appears (e.g., "skillset" for career discussion)
- Idioms used but in fewer numbers than Level 10
- Phrases like "grow to great heights" show figurative language attempt
- Word choice is precise enough to clearly communicate intended meaning

### Level 8

**Descriptor:**
- Use **common words** and **some** context-specific vocabulary to communicate meaning
- Use **some** figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use common words more precisely than lower levels and incorporate some context-specific vocabulary, figures of speech, or idioms. In Task A, the speaker uses 'professional development' and 'benefits' appropriately. In Task B, the speaker adds words or phrases such as 'best fit,' 'backrest,' and 'lay back.'"

**Observable markers for Level 8 vocabulary:**
- Context-specific terms appear but are simpler than Level 9
- Common words used with appropriate precision
- Some descriptive vocabulary specific to the topic
- Less idiomatic richness than Level 9

### Level 7

**Descriptor:**
- Use **common words** and **some more precise vocabulary** to communicate meaning
- Use **some** figures of speech and idioms

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use common words and phrases to communicate meaning with more precision than speakers at lower levels and are able to use some figures of speech or idioms. In Task A, the speaker states, 'You'll establish yourself really well in the new city.' In Task B, the speaker describes the chair as 'fancy looking' and one that will 'attract attention.'"

**Observable markers for Level 7 vocabulary:**
- Common words used with reasonable precision
- Simple figurative phrases ("fancy looking," "attract attention")
- Vocabulary supports meaning but lacks the variety of Level 8
- Few advanced or context-specialized words

### Level 6

**Descriptor:**
- Use **common words**

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use common words and phrases to describe objects, express preferences, and relate events. In Task A, the speaker states, 'if you have uh a plan to go overseas, and uh work there, so don't hesitate to grab it.' In Task B, the speaker describes the chair as having, 'an adjustable seat, and it is circular.' When describing the uniqueness of the chair, the speaker adds, 'like we get to see in the movies.'"

**Observable markers for Level 6 vocabulary:**
- Common words used with simple descriptive function
- Idiomatic phrases are basic ("don't hesitate to grab it," "like we get to see in the movies")
- Little context-specific vocabulary
- Word choice is functional but not precise

### Level 5

**Descriptor:**
- Use **common words**

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can use common words. In Task A, the speaker uses 'opportunity,' 'communications,' and 'computer system' appropriately. However, as with Level 4, speakers struggle to use vocabulary to accurately describe, express ideas, or provide reasons for their opinions."

**Observable markers for Level 5 vocabulary:**
- Vocabulary is functional but limited
- Word choice sometimes fails to capture intended meaning precisely
- No idioms or figurative language beyond very common phrases
- Speakers reach for words they know rather than precise words

## 8. Criterion 3 — Listenability

**Definition:** The listener's ease of understanding the speaker, including pronunciation, intonation, pacing, fluency, and grammatical control.

**What raters evaluate:**
- Pronunciation clarity and intelligibility
- Rhythm, pacing, and intonation
- Grammatical control (range and accuracy of structures)
- Frequency and impact of self-corrections, repetitions, and pauses
- Hesitations, interjections, and fillers

**IMPORTANT NOTE FOR AI SCORING:** Listenability is the most challenging criterion to score from text-only transcripts because it depends primarily on audio characteristics. For v1, Listenability scoring will rely on transcript-derived proxies (filler words, hesitation markers, sentence structure complexity, evidence of self-correction in the transcript) rather than direct audio analysis. The system must disclose this limitation to users and clearly state that Listenability scores are approximate.

### Level 12

**Descriptor:**
- Speak with **very good control** of a **very broad range** of complex and diverse grammatical structures
- Speak intelligibly with **consistently fluent** rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers have very good control of a very broad range of complex and diverse grammatical structures. In Both Tasks A and B, the speakers can produce extended responses and incorporate a range of complex structures (e.g., 'But after thinking about a few things, I would like to give you an advice based on why you should take this opportunity' and 'If you did fall asleep, you would probably fall off the chair')."

> "Speakers can speak intelligibly with fluent rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation."

**Observable markers for Level 12 (transcript-derivable):**
- Long, complex sentence structures with multiple clauses
- Conditional structures used naturally ("If you did fall asleep, you would...")
- Extended responses with sustained complexity
- Minimal self-correction or restart markers in transcript

### Level 11

**Descriptor:**
- Have **good control** over a **broad range** of complex and diverse grammatical structures
- Speak intelligibly with **consistently fluent** rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers have good control over a broad range of complex and diverse grammatical structures. In Task A, the speaker produces a variety of complex sentence constructions throughout the response."

> "Speakers can produce extended responses with fluent rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation, and is able to do so with few hesitations, interjections, or self-corrections."

**Observable markers for Level 11 (transcript-derivable):**
- Variety of sentence constructions visible in transcript
- Few "uh," "um," or restart markers
- Sentences are extended without breakdown
- Complex grammar (conditionals, relative clauses, embedded structures) used regularly

### Level 10

**Descriptor:**
- Speak with **good control** of a **broad range** of complex grammatical structures
- Speak intelligibly with **mostly fluent** rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers have good control over a broad range of grammatical structures. In Task A, the speaker states, 'I totally see that and I miss my family, too; but you know, it gives you...' and 'later on, when you come back ...'"

> "Speakers can speak intelligibly with mostly fluent rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation. The speaker's intonation effectively expresses a preference to take the job, and also that the other person will be missed."

**Observable markers for Level 10 (transcript-derivable):**
- Use of conjunctions and discourse markers ("but you know," "later on, when...")
- Extended sentences with multiple clauses
- Some grammatical complexity but slightly more pause/restart evidence than Level 11
- Pronunciation/intonation noted as effective by raters (audio-only marker)

### Level 9

**Descriptor:**
- Speak with **some control** of complex grammatical structures
- Speak intelligibly with **mostly fluent** rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers have control over simple grammatical structures and some complex structures. In Task A, the speaker produces several complex sentences (e.g., 'In a way it's good because if your parents come to you, you can show them around the new country, they haven't been there before')."

> "Compared to Level 8, speakers are also able to extend responses with fewer pauses and maintain understandable rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation."

**Observable markers for Level 9 (transcript-derivable):**
- Several complex sentences but interspersed with simpler ones
- Fewer pauses than Level 8 (visible in transcript as fewer hesitation markers)
- Causal and conditional structures used
- Sustained extended responses

### Level 8

**Descriptor:**
- Speak with **some control** of complex grammatical structures
- Speak intelligibly with **mostly fluent** rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation
- Speak with **very few** self-corrections, repetitions, or inappropriate pauses

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speaker have control over simple grammatical structures and some control over complex structures. In Task A, the speaker states, 'And if you miss the opportunity, later on you will regret it.' In Task B, the speaker states, 'One thing I noticed is the size of it is actually reasonable, it's not as big as you may think it is.'"

> "Speakers can also produce extended responses with fewer pauses than lower levels and speak with understandable rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation."

**Observable markers for Level 8 (transcript-derivable):**
- Simple structures dominate; complex structures appear but are less consistent
- Conditional and comparative structures used ("not as big as you may think")
- Fewer pauses than Level 7
- Some grammatical errors may persist but don't impede understanding

### Level 7

**Descriptor:**
- Speak with **good control** of simple grammatical structures
- Speak **clearly** with **understandable** rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation
- Speak with **very few** self-corrections, repetitions, or inappropriate pauses

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers have good control over simple grammatical structures. In Task A, the speaker states, 'If I were you, I would take this opportunity' and 'take this opportunity even if you feel ...' In Task B, the speaker states, 'It has a lamp at the top that can help you to read books or focus on whatever task you are doing at night.'"

> "Speakers speak clearly with understandable rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation."

> "Speakers speak with very few self-corrections, repetitions, or inappropriate pauses."

**Observable markers for Level 7 (transcript-derivable):**
- Solid command of simple structures (conditional, relative clauses)
- Few visible hesitation markers in transcript
- Sentences are clear though less complex than Level 8
- Clear, understandable delivery (audio marker but transcript shows few restarts)

### Level 6

**Descriptor:**
- Speak **with control** of simple grammatical structures
- **Usually** speak with understandable rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation
- Speak with **some** self-corrections, repetitions, or inappropriate pauses

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can communicate with more control of simple grammatical structures than lower levels. In Task A, the speaker says, 'if you like to have a good standard family living, you will uh grab that opportunity.'"

> "Speakers can usually communicate with understandable rhythm, pronunciation, and intonation with some pauses, repetitions, and self-corrections. In Task B, there are a few pauses and pronunciation issues that occasionally interfere with understanding, but this is less distracting than responses at lower levels."

**Observable markers for Level 6 (transcript-derivable):**
- Hesitation markers ("uh," "um") visible in transcript
- Self-corrections occur but don't dominate
- Simple structures generally controlled
- Some grammatical errors visible
- Pauses and repetitions begin to interfere with flow

### Level 5

**Descriptor:**
- Speak with **some control** of **simple** grammatical structures

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers have some control over simple grammatical structures. In Task B, the speaker says, 'There's a funny-looking chair here that would suit you ...'"

**Observable markers for Level 5 (transcript-derivable):**
- Simple structures only; complex structures rare or absent
- More frequent restarts and hesitations than Level 6
- Grammar errors more visible

## 9. Criterion 4 — Task Fulfillment

**Definition:** The speaker's ability to address the prompt, adapt language to the situation/audience, and convey intended meaning to the listener.

**What raters evaluate:**
- Did the response do what the prompt asked?
- Was language adapted to the relationship (friend, authority figure, formal, informal)?
- Was the intended meaning communicated effectively?
- Did the speaker situate themselves appropriately in the communicative context?

### Level 12

**Descriptor:**
- Adapt language to **the situation**, desired purposes, and relationships to listeners
- **Precisely communicate** ideas to others when speaking

**Rater Analysis:**

> "Speakers can communicate their ideas to others and adapt their language to the situation, their desired purposes, and their relationship to listeners. In Task A, the speaker indicates that he is aware of the challenges the other person is experiencing when stating, 'I know that you are a little bit concerned because it's really far from here.' In this way, the speaker assumes the role of a sympathetic advisor. In Task B, the speaker expresses excitement at finding the chair and the need to 'ring you to tell you about it.' In this way, the speaker assumes the role of a friend offering information of interest to the listener."

**Observable markers for Level 12 Task Fulfillment:**
- Speaker explicitly demonstrates awareness of listener's situation/feelings
- Adopts a clear communicative role appropriate to the task (sympathetic advisor, excited friend)
- Language register precisely matches the relationship
- Purpose of communication is clearly fulfilled

### Level 11

- **Communicate** ideas to others when speaking

## 11. Official Speaking Study Tips

These are the eight tips published by Paragon Testing on the CELPIP Free Resources page. They represent CELPIP's own guidance on what graders look for and what test-takers should prioritize.

1. **Take the time to practice speaking English.** Practice with friends, family, and co-workers. Remember the test is delivered through a computer with no human examiner — practice speaking into a recording device to build confidence.

2. **Follow the instructions and address the prompt.** Read all instructions before beginning. Use preparation time to read and understand the prompt and organize thoughts before speaking.

3. **Give complete answers.** Provide CELPIP raters with as much content as possible so they can accurately assess oral proficiency. Elaborate with relevant details. Do not go off topic, but speak as much as possible in the time allotted.

4. **Draw from your own life experiences.** Talking about something personal and meaningful is much easier than making something up.

5. **Speak clearly and at a natural pace.** Use the recording device test before the exam to find the best volume, tone, and pace.

6. **Try not to repeat words.** Use your own words to show full ability rather than repeating words from the prompt. Use synonyms and paraphrases to demonstrate vocabulary range.

7. **Don't worry about your accent.** Unless the accent prevents understanding, do not focus on it. Concentrate instead on good grammar, accurate and varied vocabulary, and thoroughly responding to prompts.

8. **Manage your time.** Use preparation time to understand the prompt, brainstorm ideas, and organize what to say. Use the speaking timer to ensure complete responses and finish the last sentence before time is up.

### Implications for AI Scoring

These tips reveal what CELPIP itself emphasizes in its rater training:

- **Accent is explicitly de-prioritized** — graders do not penalize for accent unless intelligibility is affected
- **Vocabulary range is rewarded** — repetition of prompt words is implicitly penalized
- **Task adherence matters** — going off-topic is a clear negative
- **Complete utilization of time** is encouraged but not the only factor — quality matters more than length
- **Personal/authentic content is encouraged** — implies that strained, formulaic responses score lower than genuine ones

## 12. Open Gaps and Limitations

This document captures the complete official rubric foundation available from celpip.ca. The following gaps are known and will be addressed in subsequent research phases:

### Gap 1: Sample Responses for Tasks 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8

Paragon publishes scored samples for only Task 1 (Giving Advice) and Task 3 (Describing a Scene). The other six task types do not have official scored sample data. Sources for closing this gap:

- Unofficial teardowns from CELPIP Mastery, Mighty Tips, and other CELPIP-prep YouTube channels
- Paid CELPIP Practice Tests (Paragon's own) which may contain task-specific sample responses
- The CELPIP Online Practice Test (free) may include sample Speaking responses for additional tasks

### Gap 2: Audio Calibration Data

The Sample Responses tab on celpip.ca contains audio recordings that this document does not capture. For Listenability scoring calibration (and for the v2 build of multimodal audio analysis), the actual audio files matter. Sources:

- celpip.ca Score Comparison Chart audio players (must be played, cannot be downloaded easily)
- CELPIP's official YouTube channel (Speaking-focused playlists)
- Captured during Requirement #2 (YouTube research phase)

### Gap 3: Levels 1-4 Data

This document covers Levels 5-12 only. Levels 1-4 are out of scope for v1 because:

- Users at those levels need foundational ESL instruction, not test prep
- The product's target audience aims for CLB 7-10
- Paragon publishes less detailed rubric data for these levels

If user demand for sub-CLB-5 scoring emerges, this gap can be revisited.

### Gap 4: Edge Cases and Borderline Scoring

The rubric provides clear descriptors for each integer level, but real responses often fall between levels. Paragon's actual rater training likely includes guidance on resolving borderline cases (e.g., "is this response a high 8 or a low 9?"). This guidance is not publicly available. The AI will need to be calibrated through:

- Beta tester feedback (when their self-assessed level differs from AI's score)
- Iterative tuning based on edge cases that arise in production

### Gap 5: Listenability from Text Alone

As flagged throughout Section 8: text transcripts cannot fully capture pronunciation, intonation, pacing, and audio fluency. v1 will use transcript-derived proxies (filler words, hesitation markers from Whisper timing data, sentence structure complexity) and disclose this limitation to users. v2 will explore multimodal audio scoring (GPT-4o audio, Gemini, etc.).
